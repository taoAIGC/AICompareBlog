## Why do many e-commerce websites just go online, and the product pages have not yet started to be sold, but the thank you page, order success page, and payment success page are seen by search engines first?

This is quite common in independent websites, Shopify, WooCommerce, and SaaS order systems.

Once the process is completed, these addresses will easily appear on the site:

- `/thank-you`
- `/order/success`
- `/checkout/success`
- `/thank-you?order=12345`
- `/thank-you?payment=paypal`
- `/order/complete?from=campaign`
- `/success?coupon=welcome10`
- There are even post-status pages such as payment success page, order completion page, download success page, invitation sharing page, and coupon rebate page.

Many people’s first reaction will be:

- The order has been placed successfully. This page is closest to the transaction. Is the SEO value higher?
- The thank you page can also include coupons, recommended products, and invitations to share, which may be worth using for natural ranking.
- The system generates it by default. It should not be a problem to keep it for inclusion.
- One more page is better than one less page

It doesn't sound entirely unreasonable.

But after doing it for a long time, you will find that this kind of page often looks like this:

- The content of the page is very thin. The core only contains the words "Order Successfully Placed", order number, button and jump entrance.
- The URL will change with the order status, payment method, activity parameters, and device source.
- The page often contains user order information, email, amount, and address status, which is naturally more private.
- The user clicks in from the search results and sees a status page after completing the purchase, which is not the information he really wants to know.
- Product pages, category pages, payment instructions pages, delivery instructions pages, and after-sales pages that should really receive traffic have not been strengthened.

So if you are also studying **How ​​to handle the thank you page** recently, let me give you a very direct conclusion:

**Most thank you pages are more like "status confirmation page after transaction" rather than "search request landing page". It is of course important for conversion statistics, after-sales guidance, and secondary marketing, but most of the time it is not suitable to be promoted as a key natural traffic page. What really needs to be done is not to simply release or block it across the board, but to distinguish first: whether this URL is serving users who have placed orders, or whether it is answering public search questions. **

## What problem does the thank you page solve? It doesn’t just “tell the user that the order was successful”

Many people’s understanding of thank you pages still remains at:

- The prompt page that users see after completing payment
- The last step in the conversion funnel
- An optional back page

This understanding is not wrong, but it is still too shallow.

A true thank you page usually does at least three things.

### 1. It is confirming the transaction status, not answering content questions

The core tasks of a thank you page are usually:

- Tell the user that the order has been submitted or the payment was successful
- Prompt order number, delivery information, invoice or download instructions
- Guide users to see order details, logistics, member center, and customer service entrance
- Trigger advertising attribution, conversion statistics, and remarketing scripts

In other words, it serves more "people who have completed the action" rather than "people who are still searching and comparing."

### 2. It is often strongly bound to orders, payments, and identity information

for example:

- Whether the payment has been successful
- Is it a visitor placing an order or logging in to place an order?
- Is it a virtual product or a physical product?
- Whether there are rebates, gifts, or upgrade packages
- Whether to display the order amount, delivery information, and download link

This determines that the thank you page is usually not a stable public page, but a type of process page that changes with the order status.

### 3. It has business value, but it does not mean it has SEO value.

Thank you pages are of course important.

Without it, users will feel insecure; statistics are incomplete, and the team cannot judge delivery and conversion effects.

However, it should be noted that the important business does not mean that it is suitable for undertaking natural search. **

When users search for these words:

- How to calculate shipping cost?
- How long does it take for the order to be shipped?
- Can I issue an invoice?
- What to do if payment fails
- How to apply for after-sales service
- Are there any newbie coupons?

What is really more suitable to undertake is usually not a thank you page, but:

- Product details page
- FAQ page
- Payment instructions page
- Delivery instructions page
- After-sales policy page
- Activity rules page

If these questions are finally answered by the thank you page, it is most likely not that the design is clever, but that the entrance is placed in the wrong place.

## How to deal with the thank you page? I would suggest handling these 5 situations separately first.

### 1. If it is just a standard order success page or payment success page, there is usually no need to treat it as a key SEO page.

This is the most common category.

for example:

- Standard thank you page
- Order submission success page
- Payment completion page
- Appointment submission success page
- Download the purchase completion page

This type of page is very important for business closed loop, but the problem is also obvious:

**Most of them do not exist to meet open search needs. **

After users click in from a search engine, they often only have a few questions:

- I haven’t placed an order, why am I seeing a success page?
- What is the product or service corresponding to this page?
- Where are the order details, rules, and after-sales instructions?
- Is this a public page or a process page for a certain user?

So a standard thank you page is usually more suitable as a process closing page rather than a focused SEO page.

In a word: **Thank you page can be very important, but this importance is mostly important in the sense of conversion and operation, not in the sense of natural search. **

### 2. If the real search value is logistics, after-sales, payment, and discount descriptions, don’t let the thank you page take over the traffic for them.

This misunderstanding is very common.

Some teams see a lot of visits to the thank you page and subconsciously feel:

- This page is closest to the transaction and should be more worthy of SEO
- There are coupons, recommendations, and invitation rebates on the page, and you may be able to get search traffic.
- It’s all post-transaction information anyway, so there’s no harm in accepting it.

But this is often unstable from an SEO perspective.

Because users often search for something other than:

- What does the order success page look like?
- Where is the address of the success page?

Instead:

- When will the order be shipped?
- How to deal with payment failure
- How to issue an invoice
- How to check logistics
- When will the coupons be issued?
- Is there an after-sales entrance?

These questions are more appropriately asked by:

- Help center page
- Logistics instructions page
- Payment FAQ page
- After-sales policy page
- Activity rules page
- Order tracking page

To take over.

**Don't let the page that really answers the question hide in the back, but push a status page to the front. **

### 3. If the thank you page will generate parameter URLs such as order, payment, coupon, and source, you must first distinguish between "functional URL" and "index URL"

The real problem with many website thank you pages is not that there is only one `/thank-you`, but that it also generates many variations:

- `/thank-you?order=12345`
- `/thank-you?payment=paypal`
- `/thank-you?coupon=welcome10`
- `/order/success?from=email`
- `/checkout/success?source=ads`
- `/success?download=true`

These addresses may be useful for business logic.

But for SEO, they often mean:

- A large number of near-duplicate URLs with parameters
- Fetched resources are eaten by the status page
- Page signals are broken up by different channel parameters
- The order number, source parameters, and discount status were publicly exposed

So when dealing with the thank you page, a particularly practical action is:

- First distinguish what URLs are needed for statistics, attribution, and processes
- Distinguish what pages search engines really should see
- Parametric success pages are output everywhere by default
- Don’t let order numbers, payment status, and discount parameters become public index entries

**The most common problem with the thank you page is often not the text, but the parameters and private information. **

### 4. If the thank you page is filled with coupons, recommended products, download resources, and invitation rebates, you must first determine whether it is a "module" or an "independent landing page"

This situation is becoming more and more common now.

Many teams will make the thank you page very full:

- Get a coupon after placing an order successfully
- Recommend related products
- Guide to join as a member
- Guide sharing friends to get rebates
- Provide download information or activation guide

The features themselves are fine.

But it should be noted that "the thank you page has content" does not mean "the thank you page is suitable for participating in search rankings". **

A more stable way to judge is:

- If it is just a supplementary guide after the transaction, it is still more like a process page
- If a certain piece of content has independent search requirements, it can be made into a separate topic page, rules page, and help page.
- Don’t shoehorn a bunch of marketing modules into your success page and expect it to also do SEO by the way.

Many times, the marketing components in the thank you page serve users who have made transactions, not unfamiliar search users.

### 5. If you don’t intend to let the thank you page participate in the ranking, unify noindex, sitemap, internal links, canonical, and burying logic together

Many thank you page SEO problems are not because of "do you have a thank you page", but because the signals are fighting each other.

Common situations are:

- The team says the success page is not important
- sitemap also submitted the thank you URL
- Advertising, EDM, and SMS links all directly pop up the parameter page
- The template also adds ordinary internal links to these status pages.
- canonical rules are not unified
- I was also worried that noindex would affect conversion statistics, so I kept putting off processing it technically.

Finally it becomes:

- You have no intention of letting it receive search traffic.
- Search engines keep getting hints that "this page may be important"
- The product pages, FAQ pages, and description pages that should be recommended are not concentrated enough.

If you decide not to focus on the SEO entrance for your thank you page, a more stable idea is usually:

- Maintain a clear and standard success page process
- sitemap only submits pages that really want to participate in rankings
- The parameter success page should be exposed as little as possible
- Don’t package the thank you page into a content page for internal links
- Noindex, canonical, buried points, and jump logic remain consistent

Don't fight with technical signals. This is more important than "whether the thank you page will be included or not" itself.

## The 4 most common SEO mistakes I see for thank you pages are more likely to mess up the website than not doing them.

### 1. Think of the thank you page as the page “closest to a sale, so it’s the page that should be ranked the most”

This is typical of mixing conversion logic and search logic.

Being close to a transaction means that it is suitable for serving people who have completed the action;
It does not mean that it is suitable for people who are still understanding, judging, and comparing.

### 2. Put the order number, payment status, and channel parameters directly into a publicly accessible URL

This is not just an SEO issue, but also a privacy and risk control issue.

What many successful pages fear most is not not being included, but being shared incorrectly, cached, indexed, and exposed to too much order information after being crawled.

### 3. Logistics, after-sales, and discount rules are not handled separately. Instead, the thank you page is responsible for the explanation task.

We didn't create a description page that could really answer the user's questions. As a result, the success page was turned into a mixed page that neither users nor search engines could understand.

### 4. The five sets of logics of noindex, sitemap, canonical, advertising attribution, and page jumps conflict with each other.

What you fear most is not that you choose the wrong method, but that you say it is not important, but technically you keep hinting that "it is important".

This signal fight is often more damaging than simply keeping a thank you page.

## Should the thank you page be included? Don’t ask about the “unified answer” first, ask first whether it is accepting public search requests.

Many people will still come back to the same question in the end:

**Should the thank you page be included? **

My judgment method has always been very simple. Instead of asking "how others do it", I first ask what this page is doing.

If it's mainly doing these things:

- Confirm the order is placed successfully
- Display order information
- Provide next step guidance after payment or downloading
- Trigger statistical attribution
- Provide additional purchases or membership guidance to users who have completed transactions

Then it is most likely still a function page or status page.

These types of pages can be critical to the business, but most of the time they don’t need to be a key organic search entry.

Only in rare cases, such as when the page itself has been made into a public activity results page, a resource collection description page, or a service description page that can independently meet search needs, can it be more than just a traditional thank you page.

But if it’s just a standard success page, don’t force it to memorize SEO KPIs.

## Last sentence

So if you are still struggling with **how to deal with the thank you page**, here is my advice:

**Think of the thank you page as a "post-transaction status page" first, and then decide whether it should be included in the index; don't just put a bunch of success pages with order information and parameters into the search results just because the system generates them by default. **
