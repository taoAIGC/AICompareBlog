# How to understand search intent? Don’t start writing right away — sort out these 4 intent types first if you want better traffic

## Why do some articles get long and detailed, yet still attract the wrong traffic?

A lot of SEO problems don’t start at the writing stage.

They start earlier — when the topic is aimed in the wrong direction.

If someone searches for “how to write SEO titles,” they probably want methods, templates, and examples.
If someone searches for “Ahrefs vs Semrush,” they are already comparing options.
If you give the first person a basic article about “what SEO is,” or give the second person a beginner explainer about SEO tools, the mismatch shows up immediately.

That mismatch is search intent.

In plain words, search intent means this:

**What is the user actually trying to get done when they type that keyword?**

Sometimes they want to learn.
Sometimes they want to find a specific site.
Sometimes they want to compare tools.
Sometimes they are already close to signing up or paying.

If you don’t figure that out first, your title, structure, CTA, and internal links can all end up going the wrong way.

---

## What search intent really means

Search intent is not just about the words on the screen.

It is about the need behind those words.

Two people can search similar keywords while being in completely different stages:

- one is still learning
- one is trying to find a page
- one is comparing before buying
- one is ready to act

That’s why I don’t judge a keyword by search volume alone.
Before I write, I usually run through four questions:

1. **Does this user want an answer, or do they want to make a decision?**
2. **Are they in the learning stage, comparison stage, or action stage?**
3. **What kind of pages already rank on the first page?**
4. **If I give them the wrong format, will they bounce right away?**

Those four questions are more useful than a lot of fancy keyword spreadsheets.

## I usually group search intent into these 4 types

This is not a perfect academic model, but for real content work it is practical enough.

### 1. Informational intent: the user wants to understand something

These keywords often contain words like:

- how to
- what is
- tutorial
- guide
- why
- examples

Examples:

- how to write SEO titles
- what is search intent
- how to find long-tail keywords
- why is my site not indexed

This kind of keyword works best with:

- tutorials
- guides
- step-by-step articles
- practical breakdowns

If the user is still trying to understand the topic, pushing a product page too early usually won’t work.

### 2. Navigational intent: the user already knows where they want to go

Here the user is not trying to learn. They want to reach a brand, a site, a tool, or a specific page.

Examples:

- Google Search Console login
- Ahrefs homepage
- AI Compare extension

These keywords usually fit:

- homepages
- login pages
- feature pages
- brand pages

A blog post can sometimes rank here, but in many cases the official page is what users actually want.

### 3. Commercial investigation: the user is comparing options

This is one of the best areas for SEO content because the need already exists, but the decision is not final yet.

Common patterns:

- which is better
- vs
- best
- review
- recommended
- worth it

Examples:

- ChatGPT vs Claude
- Ahrefs vs Semrush
- best free SEO tools
- is this AI writing tool worth it

Good content formats here are:

- comparison articles
- tool roundups
- pros and cons breakdowns
- “best for” recommendations

### 4. Transactional intent: the user is close to acting

These keywords are closest to conversion.
They often include:

- price
- sign up
- buy
- download
- coupon
- free trial

Examples:

- SEO tool pricing
- AI plugin download
- Ahrefs coupon
- free trial for a tool

At this stage, users usually want:

- clear pricing
- feature differences
- setup requirements
- direct access to the next step

If you make them scroll through a long generic introduction first, many will leave.

## When I judge search intent, I look at these 4 places first

### Method 1: Look for action words in the keyword

A lot of intent is visible in the modifier itself.

“How to” and “what is” usually point to informational intent.
“Best,” “review,” and “which is better” often point to commercial investigation.
“Pricing,” “download,” and “sign up” are usually much closer to transactional intent.

These small words matter more than many people think.

### Method 2: Search the keyword and study the results page

This is the method I trust most.

Search the term yourself and look at the top results.
Usually the first page tells you what Google believes users want.

Pay attention to:

- whether the ranking pages are guides, product pages, homepages, or reviews
- which words keep showing up in titles
- whether the content is helping users solve a problem or pushing them toward a decision
- whether you see patterns like “best,” “vs,” or “pricing” everywhere

If the results page is full of tutorials, trying to rank a sales page will be much harder.

### Method 3: Check whether one keyword actually contains multiple stages

Some keywords are mixed.

For example, “AI writing tools” may attract people who want a definition, a comparison list, or pricing information.

That is why one giant page trying to cover everything often feels messy.

A better approach is usually to split it:

- one article for tool recommendations
- one article for how to choose
- one page for pricing or free plans

Separating the stages often brings cleaner traffic.

### Method 4: Ask what the user wants to do right after clicking

Search intent affects conversion too, not just ranking.

If someone searches “how to write SEO descriptions,” what they really want after clicking is probably this:

**Understand it, apply it, and use it right away.**

So your page should quickly provide:

- decision rules
- common mistakes
- usable templates

Not a long warm-up full of background talk.

## These 3 mistakes make content drift off target fast

### Mistake 1: Looking only at the keyword, not the stage

A beginner searching “SEO tools” and a buyer searching “SEO tools pricing” are not in the same moment.

### Mistake 2: Guessing intent without checking the search results

A lot of people imagine what users want without validating it. That usually ends with content that feels original, but does not match the market.

### Mistake 3: Trying to cover everything in one article

If one page tries to rank for “what is,” “best,” “pricing,” and “download” at the same time, it usually loses focus.

My rule is simple:

**One page should serve one main intent first.**

---

## If you need to write today, use this simple workflow

### Step 1: Define the user’s stage
Are they learning, comparing, or ready to act?

### Step 2: Search the keyword yourself
Don’t skip this. The results page is the fastest reality check.

### Step 3: Choose the right content format
Tutorial, listicle, comparison, feature page, pricing page — don’t mix them carelessly.

### Step 4: Write the title and outline after that
Intent first, title second. If you reverse the order, you often keep rewriting everything.

### Step 5: Do one final intent check
Ask yourself:

**If I were the person searching this keyword, would this page feel like exactly what I wanted right now?**

If the answer is shaky, the page probably still needs work.

## When I use AI to help with content, I add one extra step

AI is great for outlining, headline ideas, and alternative angles.

But it has a common weakness:

**It loves mixing stages together.**

You ask for “best free SEO tools,” and it gives you a definition, a tutorial, a pricing section, and a product pitch all in one draft.

That can look complete, but it is not focused.

So I usually define the keyword, audience, and stage first, then compare different model outputs for structure. Sometimes I use **AI Compare** to look at multiple answers side by side — not to copy them, but to check:

- is this outline drifting into the wrong intent?
- is this title pulling the user toward a different stage?
- is this draft helping the reader understand, compare, or buy?

That tiny step saves a lot of rewriting later.

---

## Final thought

Search intent sounds like an SEO term, but it is actually very human.

Just remember this:

**Give users what they want now, not what you want to push first.**

If they are still looking for answers, don’t rush to sell.
If they are ready to act, don’t trap them in a long beginner lecture.

A lot of content underperforms not because the writing is bad, but because the page never matched the user’s current stage.

So before you write your next article, pause for a minute.
Search the keyword.
Look at the results page.
Then ask yourself:

**What is this person really trying to do right now?**

Once that becomes clear, the title, structure, and conversion path usually get much easier.
