## Why do so many people think a sitemap should include every page they can find?

I see this mistake all the time.

A site goes live, someone starts taking SEO seriously, and the first reaction is usually:

- generate a sitemap
- throw every URL into it
- submit it in Search Console
- wait for indexing and rankings

It feels efficient.

But in practice, **a sitemap is not better because it is bigger. It is better because it is cleaner.**

If you put test pages, parameter URLs, duplicate pages, thin pages, and noindex pages into the same sitemap, you are not telling search engines “my site is complete.” You are telling them “I have not cleaned up my own structure yet.”

So if you are working on how to submit a sitemap, here is the short version:

**The real job is not to submit more URLs. The real job is to submit the right URLs.**

---

## What does a sitemap actually do? Don’t treat it like a forced-indexing button

A sitemap is basically a list.

You are telling search engines:

> These are the URLs I consider official and worth checking first.

That means “please look at these first,” not “you must index these immediately.”

A sitemap is useful because it can:

1. help search engines discover pages faster
2. show which URLs are the preferred versions
3. force you to organize your own site structure

It cannot replace content quality, internal linking, or search intent.

If the page is thin, repetitive, or technically conflicting, a sitemap will only expose the problem faster.

## How to submit a sitemap? I’d rather get these 5 details right first

### 1. Only submit the canonical, index-worthy URLs you truly want indexed

This is the biggest one.

My own rule is simple:

**If this page got indexed today, would I feel it deserves to be there?**

If the answer is no, I do not put it in the sitemap.

That usually means keeping out:

- tracking-parameter URLs
- internal search result pages
- preview and test pages
- obvious duplicate tag or filter pages
- URLs that canonicalize to another page

What should go in instead:

- final article pages
- final product pages
- main category pages
- landing pages you genuinely want to rank

---

### 2. On a new site, submit the core pages first

A lot of new sites do not have a sitemap problem. They have a thin-page problem.

When a site is still young, I would rather submit:

- the homepage
- core category pages
- the most important articles
- pages that are already complete and well structured

Let search engines build a clean first impression.

Once those pages are getting crawled and indexed steadily, you can expand the sitemap gradually.

---

### 3. Don’t fake lastmod dates

This detail gets ignored a lot.

Some systems stamp every URL in the sitemap with today’s date, even when nothing meaningful changed. That looks active, but it is not helpful.

Over time, search engines may just stop trusting it.

A safer approach is:

- update lastmod when the page content really changed
- do not touch it for tiny cosmetic edits
- do not give every URL the exact same timestamp

Accurate signals are always better than noisy signals.

---

### 4. Split large sitemaps by type instead of dumping everything into one file

When the site grows, I prefer splitting sitemaps by content type.

For example:

- articles
- products
- categories
- language versions

This helps because:

1. it makes issues easier to debug
2. it lets you see which type of content is being handled well and which is not

If everything is mixed together, troubleshooting becomes much slower.

---

### 5. After submission, watch the feedback

A lot of people only do the first half: submit.

The useful part is the second half: **check what happened afterward.**

I usually look at:

- whether the submitted URL count matches reality
- whether “discovered, not indexed” keeps piling up
- whether a large number of submitted URLs are excluded
- whether the sitemap conflicts with canonical, noindex, or robots rules

If the sitemap is valid but the pages still do not get indexed, the problem is usually not the submission itself.

---

## What pages do I usually keep out of a sitemap?

I normally exclude:

- 404 or 410 pages
- redirected URLs
- noindex pages
- URLs canonicalized elsewhere
- obvious parameter duplicates
- internal search, cart, login, and other utility pages
- placeholder pages that are not finished yet

A simple rule:

**Do not formally hand search engines pages you do not even want to showcase.**

---

## If the sitemap is submitted and indexing is still slow, check these 4 things first

### 1. Is the page worth indexing at all?

That is still the core issue.

Thin content, vague intent, and weak structure will not be fixed by a sitemap.

### 2. Does the page have internal entry points?

Many pages do not fail because they were not submitted. They fail because nothing inside the site leads to them.

Check for:

- internal links
- category placement
- navigation access
- contextual recommendations

### 3. Are your technical signals fighting each other?

This is one of the most common technical SEO problems.

For example:

- the sitemap submits URL A
- canonical points to URL B
- robots blocks part of the path
- the page also has noindex

If you send mixed signals, search engines will hesitate.

### 4. Is crawling or the server unstable?

If the site is slow, returns errors, or performs badly on mobile, search engines will be more conservative.

Submitting the sitemap again and again will not fix that.

---

## One last thing

Submitting a sitemap is not complicated.

The biggest mistake is not “not knowing how to generate one.” It is treating it like a magic fix whenever indexing feels slow.

My process is simple:

- clean up the URLs first
- submit only the official pages
- check the feedback
- if there is a problem, go back to the page quality and site structure

Use your sitemap as a clean, trustworthy list of pages that matter.

That is when it actually starts helping.
