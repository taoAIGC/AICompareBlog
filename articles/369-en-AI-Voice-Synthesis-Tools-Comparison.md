## Why are more and more people starting to use AI dubbing?

The reason is actually very simple: **Real-person recording is too time-consuming**.

Those who make short videos have to record oral broadcasts repeatedly; those who make courses have to fill in the sound one by one; those who make podcasts and audio content are most afraid of finding a mispronunciation of a sentence after recording and having to start over again. Not to mention that some teams still have to find dubbing actors, make appointments, and change lines. Once the process becomes long, the efficiency will be lost.

At this time, the value of AI speech synthesis comes out.

It is not just about "speaking out the words", but has begun to approach a truly usable dubbing tool: speech speed can be adjusted, emotions can be controlled, multiple languages ​​can be cut, and some products can even do voice cloning. For many ordinary creators, this is no longer a toy, but a productivity tool that can directly save time and costs.

I recently tried 5 common AI speech synthesis tools in a row to see if they "sound like robots" or if they have reached the stage of "can be used to publish works directly".

---

## How did I test it?

Instead of just saying "Hello, welcome" to fool the test, I prepared 3 sets of copywriting that are closer to the real workflow:

1. **Product introduction oral broadcast**: Test whether the pronunciation will be messy when the information density is high
2. **Knowledge explanation narration**: Test whether the pauses and rhythm are natural in long texts
3. **Emotional Sentences**: Test whether it can pronounce some real emotional ups and downs

I mainly look at 5 dimensions:

- **Is it natural enough**
- **Chinese pronunciation is smooth or not**
- **How ​​is the stability of long text**
- **Optional sound and cloning ability are strong or not**
- **Are the price and entry threshold acceptable**

Many tools are okay for listening to the first sentence, but once it is extended to one minute, all the problems will appear: some sentence breaks are weird, some emotions are always flat, and some English brand names in Chinese are overturned.

So what I care more about this time is not "whether I can make a sound", but whether I can actually use it to work.

## 1. ElevenLabs: If you are looking for "real person", it is still very capable

**Usage experience:**

The most intuitive advantage of tools like ElevenLabs is that when you click play for the first time, you usually have a feeling of "oh, this time it really doesn't sound like the AI ​​accent before".

Especially in English and multi-language scenes, its pauses, stresses, and emotional ups and downs are really mature. When reading advertisements, telling stories, and doing video narration, the feeling of "like someone is talking seriously" is very obvious.

When I used it to play Chinese spoken broadcasts, the overall performance was good, especially the rhythm control was more natural than many old TTS. However, if there are too many Chinese Internet words, brand names, and English abbreviations mixed in the copy, it will still occasionally feel a little bit "excessive" and you need to manually adjust it.

**Suitable scene:**
- YouTube / overseas short video dubbing
- Audiobooks, podcast narration
- Multilingual content overseas
- Commercial content that requires relatively high sound quality

**shortcoming:**
- The price is not cheap
- The threshold for domestic use is relatively high
- Chinese is not its most dominant home ground

**My suggestion:** If you want to make **overseas-oriented, multi-language, hard-of-hearing content**, ElevenLabs is still worth trying. Especially when it comes to English content, the level of completion is really high.

## 2. Azure AI Speech: Enterprises and developers will love it more

**Usage experience:**

Azure AI Speech gives me the feeling that it is not the "most amazing", but the "most stable".

It is very similar to the kind of tool you would feel confident using when building a business system: the interface is mature, the documentation is complete, the sound library is sufficient, and the parameters are relatively detailed. If you want to make customer service announcements, app readings, car audio, and educational products, stable players like this are more reliable.

Its advantage is not entirely in "sounding most like a real person", but in **easy access, easy management, and easy expansion**. For enterprises, this is more important than whether a single trial is surprising or not.

The Chinese pronunciation is generally online, and the English and multilingual pronunciation are also relatively stable and not easy to overturn. But if you are a content creator looking for particularly impactful narration, its voice will be a little more "regular".

**Suitable scene:**
- Enterprise voice broadcast
- Educational products, reading products
- App/website TTS access
- Developers prototype voice functions

**shortcoming:**
- Emotional expression is relatively restrained
- Requires a little technical understanding of costs
- For ordinary creators, the backend will appear to be engineering-oriented.

**My suggestion:** If you are a **developer, product manager, enterprise team**, Azure AI Speech will be more practical than many "sounding cool" tools. It's not the flashiest, but it's often the most hassle-free.

## 3. Fish Audio: It is easier for Chinese creators to fall in love with it.

**Usage experience:**

Fish Audio has been easily mentioned repeatedly in the past two years, and one important reason is: **It really has something to offer in terms of Chinese and sound cloning**.

When I tried it, the most obvious feeling was that the sound style was closer to the "real person feeling" common on today's content platforms - not a traditional broadcast tone, nor a particularly deliberate emotional performance, but more like a real creator speaking.

It is generally smoother than many overseas tools in terms of Chinese copywriting, especially some oral expressions and short sentence rhythms, which are not easy to sound confusing. As for sound cloning, if the material quality is good enough, the result will be more presentable than I expected.

But the problem with it is also very real: **not every sound model is stable**. Some sounds are natural as soon as you hear them, and some sounds start to float after a long period of time. Therefore, when using it, it is best not to just read the official sample sounds, but to try out several sets of texts yourself.

**Suitable scene:**
- Chinese oral broadcast
- Self-media narration
- Sound cloning experiment
- I want to make dubbing content with a more "human touch"

**shortcoming:**
- The performance difference between models is relatively large
- Some scenes also require you to adjust the text and pauses yourself
- It is best to do a complete audition before commercial use

**My suggestion:** If what you care about most is **Chinese naturalness + sound cloning gameplay**, Fish Audio is worth putting into the first echelon to try.

## 4. iFlytek: In domestic office and course scenarios, it is really trouble-free

**Usage experience:**

The advantage of iFlytek is simple: **It is too friendly to Chinese users**.

There is no need to go through complicated configurations or study a bunch of English parameters first. Once you open it, you can select the anchor, post copy, adjust the speaking speed, and export audio. If you are doing courseware, corporate promotion, internal training, government affairs explanations, etc., it will be very convenient.

I actually listened to it and found that it has a lot of anchor styles, and the "formality" is relatively stable. When doing knowledge explanations, courseware dubbing, and promotional video narration, it is easy to quickly produce a version that is acceptable or even good.

Of course, if you want a particularly strong emotional expression and a real-life sense of language, it may not necessarily be the best one. It is more like a tool that prioritizes efficiency and is delivery-friendly.

**Suitable scene:**
- Course explanation
- Corporate video
- Report video narration
- Regular dubbing needs of domestic teams

**shortcoming:**
- The international language scene is not a strong point
- The emotional level is not as delicate as top overseas tools
- The style is more "standard broadcast"

**My suggestion:** If the core of your content is **Chinese, fast delivery, and stable process**, iFlytek is really suitable for your work.

## 5. Editing AI dubbing: the fastest to get started, and the most suitable for "do it first and then talk about it"

**Usage experience:**

The biggest advantage of AI dubbing in editing is just one sentence: **You hardly need to learn it, you can start using it immediately. **

Many people come into contact with AI dubbing for the first time, whether they are doing podcasts or complex commercial projects, or they simply want to add a spoken word to a short video. At this time, the experience of editing, a tool that is directly integrated into the editing process, will be much more comfortable than finding a separate TTS platform.

After I tested it, it was very convenient for making short video explanations, planting grass, and sharing daily knowledge. Copy the copy, choose a timbre, change the speaking speed, and the film will be ready in a few minutes.

But its problems are also obvious: if you start pursuing finer pauses, a more realistic breathing feeling, and more stable long narration, it will slowly hit the ceiling. To put it bluntly, it is a tool that is particularly suitable for getting started, but may not be suitable for achieving the ultimate goal.

**Suitable scene:**
- Douyin/Little Red Book/Video account short video
- Novice oral broadcasting
- temporary fill
- Cut content quickly

**shortcoming:**
- Long text performance is average
- Limited advanced customization capabilities
- The sound quality is more "adequate" than "amazing"

**My suggestion:** If what you need most now is **fast** instead of studying a bunch of parameters, AI dubbing for editing is really enough.

## Where will AI speech synthesis go in 2026?

My own judgment is that the next four changes will be particularly obvious:

1. **"Resembling a real person" will become a basic ability**
From now on, what everyone will be comparing will not just be whether they resemble each other, but to what extent they resemble each other and whether they can be maintained stably.

2. **Voice cloning will become more popular, but compliance will be stricter**
The technical threshold is falling, but issues such as licensing, copyright, image and voice rights will become increasingly important.

3. **Video editing and dubbing will be tied more tightly**
In the future, many people will not necessarily open a TTS tool separately, but will directly generate, replace, and modify sounds in the editing software.

4. **Chinese scenes will continue to be rolled out**
Domestic tools have made rapid progress in the past two years, especially in scenarios such as short videos, e-commerce, courses, and live broadcast slicing, and the experience will become more and more local.
