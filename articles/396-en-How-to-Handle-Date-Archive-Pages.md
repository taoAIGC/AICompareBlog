## Why is it that when many websites open a blog system, the first thing that pops up is not traffic, but a bunch of archive pages by year and month?

This is particularly common in WordPress, Typecho, Ghost, and many older CMSs.

Once the system is installed and more articles are published, these pages will slowly appear on the site:

- `/2026/`
- `/2026/03/`
- `/2026/03/page/2/`
- `/2025/12/`

When many people see this structure for the first time, they will default to:

- Isn't this nice? The website looks more complete.
- With a few more archive pages, maybe we can get more included.
- Users who want to see old content can also browse by time
- Don’t search engines like websites with a clear structure?

It all sounds reasonable.

But if you actually do it for a long time, you will find that many date archive pages will end up looking like this:

- The page title is only "March 2026"
- There is almost no text on the page. Below is a list of articles.
- There is a high degree of duplication of content between months
- Some months only 1 or 2 articles in total
- Category pages, tag pages, home page pagination, and the contents have been listed once

The result is:

- Users may not necessarily go shopping by month
- Search engines may not necessarily think that this page has independent value
- There are a lot of thin list pages on the site.

So if you are also studying **How ​​to deal with date archive pages** recently, let me give you a very straightforward conclusion:

**Date archive pages are not naturally included or blocked. The key depends on the dimension of time and whether it is a real browsing entrance for your site. If users are looking for content by year and month, then it has value; if it is just a time list page generated by the system, it is often not worthy of being a key SEO page. **

## What problem does the date archive page solve? It’s not just “listing articles by month again”

Many people’s understanding of the date archive page is still at:

- Blog system comes with pages
- Entrance to old articles on the website
- A list page sorted by time

This understanding cannot be wrong, but it is still too shallow.

A truly valuable date archive page addresses at least three things.

### 1. Give users an entrance to review content by time

On some sites, users really care about time.

for example:

- News information station
- Industry News Station
- Activity review station
- Update log site
- Content media that publishes frequently

In this type of site, users will be concerned about:

- What happened this month
- What content was posted at a certain time last year
- What has been updated before and after a certain event?
- Is the information from a certain period of time outdated?

At this time, time itself is a filtering condition.

### 2. Help the website organize frequently updated content

If a site publishes a lot of content a day, or a batch of special content is published in a month, then the date archive page may indeed become a convenient way to organize it.

Especially in information stations, media stations, and announcement stations, the time dimension is often more important than the label dimension.

### 3. Give search engines a complementary structural signal

Note here that it is "supplementary".

In other words, the date archive page can sometimes help search engines understand the time period in which the content was published, but it is usually not the core protagonist in the website structure.

The real core entrance of most websites is still:

- Category page
- Topic page
- Core articles
- Product page
- Service page

The date archive page is more like supplementary navigation rather than the main entrance to traffic.

## How to deal with the date archive page? I would suggest handling these 5 situations separately first.

### 1. If your site naturally has the need to "browse by time", the date archive page can be reserved as the official entrance.

Let’s start with the category that is most suitable for retention.

If your website has obvious time consumption attributes, such as:

- Watch news on the news station monthly
- Company Announcement Station Check historical announcements by year
- Event review site to find past events by time
- Update log site to view changes based on version release time

This type of page is not just a "list page", but actually meets user needs.

In this case, the date archive page can be completely retained, and even a layer of optimization can be carefully performed:

- Don’t just write “March 2026” in the page title
- Can you add a sentence about what are the main updates during this time period?
- If the content is large, it can be divided into topics or highlighted recommendations
- Let users know whether this archive page is worth scrolling down.

To put it bluntly, when time itself is the user's search logic, the date archive page has independent value. **

---

### 2. If it is just an ordinary blog, SaaS content site, or enterprise site, many date archive pages are not suitable as key SEO pages.

The most common problems with this type of site are:

When there are too many articles, the system automatically releases the year and month archives, but users actually don’t find content this way.

Users are more likely to search for:

- How to solve a certain problem
- What articles are there under a certain topic?
- How to use a certain tool
- What tutorials are there in a certain category?

Few people will specifically search for:

- Blog post for March 2026
- Tutorial archive for November 2025

So for ordinary content sites, the date archive page is often just:

- Overlaps with home page pagination
- Overlaps with category pages and tag pages
- Overlaps the search results page
- Not much additional information per se

In this case, it’s usually not worth promoting as a focus SEO page.

That’s not to say that it can’t exist at all, but don’t assume that it should be included on a large scale.

---

### 3. If there is very little content in many months, or even only one or two articles on a whole page, don’t let them be released in batches as thin pages.

This problem is extremely common.

Many websites may publish dozens of articles a year, and on average, they do not contain much content every month.

As a result, the system still generates:

- Annual archive page
- Monthly archive page
- Even daily archive pages

In the end, this style of painting will appear:

- Only 1 article in a given month
- Only 2 articles in a given month
- Only one sparse page of old content from a given year
- The page itself contains nothing but dates and lists

When there are too many such pages, the most likely problems are:

- More thin pages throughout the site
- Crawl resources are scattered
- There are more duplicate lists in the site
- The signal of important pages is diluted instead

Therefore, for the date archive page, don’t just look at “whether the system can generate it”, but also “whether it is worth seeing by people and search engines after it is generated.”

In many cases, having fewer monthly archive pages is more stable than releasing all of them.

---

### 4. The date archive page, category page, tag page, and home page pagination must be divided into divisions. Don’t use four sets of lists to grab the same batch of content at the same time.

This is also the tipping point I see most often.

The same batch of articles may appear on the site at the same time:

- Home page pagination
- Category page
- tab page
- Date archive page

If these pages all look similar and the only difference is the way they are sorted, it will be easy for search engines to be confused:

**Which page do you want to represent this batch of content? **

A more stable approach is usually:

- **Category Page** Responsible for subject classification
- **Tab** is responsible for horizontal supplementation and association
- **Home Page** Responsible for browsing recent updates
- **Date Archive Page** only undertakes time entry when there is indeed time to browse the needs

In one sentence:

**Don’t let the date archive page do the work of the category page, and don’t let it get mixed up with the home page pagination. **

---

### 5. If you do not plan to focus on the date archive page, unify the index, entrance, and sitemap signals.

The real problem for many teams is not whether to leave a date archive page, but that the signals are extremely messy.

A common situation is this:

- The team feels that the date archive page is not important
- But everything is put in the sitemap
- A large number of links can be added to the template
- The page itself does not have any supplementary content

This will become:

- You didn’t even intend to use it as a focus page
- Search engines keep getting signals that "this page may be important"
- Finally, crawling, inclusion, and weight distribution all become more ambiguous.

So if you decide not to focus on the date archive page, a more stable idea is usually:

- Don’t cram all year and month archives into sitemap
- Don’t give too much weight at the entrance to the station.
- Template strategy and index strategy remain consistent
- What should really be promoted are category pages, topic pages, and core article pages.

Don't push it all over the place while saying it's not important.

## The 4 most common SEO errors for date archive pages that I see now are more likely to mess up the site than not doing them.

### 1. Yearly archives, monthly archives, and daily archives are all public by default.

This is the most typical "I will put whatever the system allows".

The problem is that many websites simply do not have such a high update frequency, and releasing so many layers of archives will only make the list page thinner and thinner.

### 2. There are only dates and article lists on the page, but we expect it to get traffic.

If when a page is opened, in addition to the words "March 2026", there are only links to a few old articles, then it is difficult for it to have real independent value.

### 3. Date archiving is also superimposed with paging, filtering, and parameter pages, and finally a bunch of low-value URLs are combined

for example:

- `/2026/03/page/2/`
- `/2026/03/?sort=popular`
- `/2026/03/?tag=seo`

If there are too many such combinations, the site will easily change from "structured" to "overwhelmed with structure".

### 4. While changing the release time, while relying on time archiving for structure, the archiving system was finally messed up.

Some websites like to change the time of articles in batches, repost old articles, and backfill historical content.

This is of course not necessarily wrong, but if you rely heavily on date archiving as an entry point, the archiving itself can easily become unstable.

The time path that users see will become increasingly awkward.

## If you want to check the date archive page settings of the website now, I recommend going through them in this order.

### Step one: First take stock of which time archive pages there are in your site.

At least distinguish:

- Annual archive page
- Monthly archive page
- Daily archive page
- Pagination page under date archive

See the scale clearly first, so that you won’t miss a large area of ​​automatically generated pages while optimizing later.

### Step 2: Ask yourself a core question - will users really look for content by time?

Think clearly about the key points:

- Is time an important browsing dimension for this site?
-Does the user have the habit of "reviewing the content of a certain month"?
- Is time archiving closer to real needs than classification and labeling?

If the answer is mostly no, then the date archive page generally should not be regarded as a key SEO entry.

### Step 3: See if these pages themselves have independent value

Highlights:

- Is the content density of each archive page sufficient?
- Does the page have a basic introduction?
- Whether to just list articles repeatedly
- Are similar pages highly similar?

### Step 4: Unify sitemap, internal link entrance and index strategy

Highlights:

- Should I archive the date into the sitemap?
- Is there too much entrance in the site navigation?
- Are the archive page and category page competing for the same type of words?
- Are there a bunch of automatic pages that you don't want to push but are released?

### Step 5: Observe the actual performance, don’t just look at “how many archive pages are included”

What you really should look at is:

-Whether the user will continue to click in and browse?
- Do these pages bring real search traffic?
-Whether it affects the crawling and inclusion of core pages
- Is the overall website structure clearer rather than more confusing?

## Last sentence

The really difficult part of the date archive page is not whether it can be technically generated, but that you have to think clearly first:

- Time is not the real entrance to this site
- Are these pages helping users find content, or are they just repeating lists?
- Who is responsible for what between it and the home page pagination, category pages, and tag pages?
- Does the page itself have enough information to support "independent existence"?

If you think about these issues clearly, the date archive page will become a bounded auxiliary structure.

Otherwise, many websites will just end up with a batch of "lists of old articles arranged by year and month": users may not read them, and search engines may not pay attention to them.

So if you are still struggling with **how to deal with the date archive page**, here is my suggestion:

**Don’t think about releasing every year and month directory first. First confirm whether the time dimension is meaningful to your users, and then decide which archive pages are worth keeping and which ones are only suitable for low-key existence. **
