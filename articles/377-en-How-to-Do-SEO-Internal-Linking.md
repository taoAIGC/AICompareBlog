## Why do many websites write a lot of articles, but the traffic still flows "alone" one by one?

Many people who do SEO will carefully write titles, change descriptions, and research keywords.

None of this is a problem.

But after publishing too many articles, it is especially easy to overlook one thing: **Every article is like an isolated island. **

Today I will write an article "How to write SEO titles", tomorrow I will write an article "How to find long-tail keywords", and the day after tomorrow I will write an article "How to judge search intent". Each article is okay when viewed individually, but from the perspective of the entire site, there is almost no real connection between the articles.

The result is:

- Search engines can see a single piece of content, but it is difficult for them to understand the theme structure of your website.
- Users leave after reading this article, not knowing what to read next
- Some old articles are obviously relevant, but they never receive the traffic brought by new content.

Many people think that internal links are just "adding a few links conveniently".

Not really.

**If internal links are done well, they are essentially helping search engines find their way and also helping users avoid detours. **

To put it bluntly, SEO internal links are not about stuffing blue words into articles, but connecting the content on the site into a map that can be navigated.

---

## What problem does SEO internal linking solve?

When I look at a piece of content now and see if I should add an internal link, I don’t ask first, “Can I add another link?”, but I first ask:

**In this position, what else do users most likely want to know next? **

There are usually 3 places where internal links are really useful:

1. **Helping search engines understand the relationship between pages faster**
2. **String single pieces of content into topic clusters instead of writing each one individually**
3. **Let users move from one question to the next question**

For example, if someone is reading "How to find long-tail keywords", he will most likely be concerned about:

- How to determine the search intent after finding the word
- Where should keywords be placed on the page?
- How to write the title and description

At this time, if you can follow the path in the user's mind and connect related articles, the stickiness of the entire site's content will be obviously different.

So the internal link is not a decoration, it is more like a transportation system between content.

## I usually use these 4 questions first to determine whether this place should add internal links.

Not every word is worthy of a link.

I will go through these 4 questions now:

### 1. When users see this, do they really want to continue?
If there is no need to "continue to learn" in the context, then the link is most likely forced.

### 2. Is the linked page strongly related to the current paragraph?
Just because you want to promote a certain article, doesn’t mean it’s the right place to put it here.

### 3. After clicking here, can you add something that is not finished in the current content?
Good internal links supplement information; poor internal links just interrupt reading.

### 4. If this link is deleted, will this content be smoother?
If the answer is "yes," don't add it.

The problem with many internal links is not that the number is small, but that most people only think about SEO and not about readers. **

## I think these 5 internal linking ideas are much more effective than "linking as soon as you see a word"

### Idea 1: Chain from "current problem" to "next step problem"

This is the one I use most.

For example, when a user is looking at "How to determine search intent", the next step is often:

- Is this word worth writing?
- How should the title start?
- How to lay out the content

At this time, internal linking is very natural, because it follows the user's train of thought.

This type of link is the most comfortable and easiest.

---

### Idea 2: Chain from "general problems" to "more specific problems"

Some articles are suitable for entry pages and talk about larger topics.

For example, "How to do SEO keyword layout", this kind of content can naturally link to:

- How to put keywords in the title
- How to write a description
- How to find long-tail keywords
- How to determine search intent

The former is responsible for clarifying the general direction, and the latter is responsible for breaking down specific actions.

The function of this type of internal link is to slowly break down "a big topic" into a series of detailed questions, which is not only convenient for search engines to understand, but also for users to continue reading.

---

### Idea 3: Link from "Tutorial Page" to "Comparison Page" or "Selection Page"

Many users just want to learn the method at first, but halfway through they start to think:

**So what exactly should I use? **

For example, in an article about "How to write SEO titles", it is very suitable to continue after reading:

- Recommended title generation tools
- AI SEO tool comparison
- Which tools are right for content teams

This kind of link does not force the goods, but adds "how to choose" in the content path.

As long as the relevance is high enough, users will not be disgusted.

---

### Idea 4: From the concept word chain to the explanation page, don’t assume everyone understands it

When many people write articles, they assume that readers know what search intent, long-tail keywords, anchor text, and topic clusters are.

The reality is usually not like this.

So when you encounter these key concepts, if you happen to have a corresponding explanation page on your site, you can naturally introduce them when they first appear.

But be careful not to repeat the link every time it appears in an article.

**For the same concept, it is usually enough to give it a sign when it first appears. **

---

### Idea 5: Focus on “extended reading” at the end to help users continue on.

Many people end the article by writing a summary.

In fact, the end is a very suitable place to put internal links, because at this time the user has read the entire article and it is easiest to decide whether to continue reading the next article.

What I recommend more is not to throw out a row of "related articles", but to give them according to relationship:

- If you want to choose words first, you can follow "How to Find Long Tail Keywords"
- If you want to determine demand, you can pick up "How to determine search intent"
- If you want to put the words in the right position, you can pick up "How to Make SEO Keyword Layout"
- If you want to increase clicks, you can pick up "How to Write an SEO Title" or "How to Write an SEO Description"

This way of writing will be more like recommendations from real people, not like modules automatically spelled out by the system.

## How to write anchor text so that it doesn’t look like you are trying to do SEO?

Many people do internal linking, not because of "whether there is a link", but because the anchor text is too perfunctory.

The most common ones:

- Click here
- View details
- Related reading
- More content

Of course, this kind of word can also jump, but it hardly tells search engines and users what the click is. **

A more natural way of writing is usually to directly use question words, topic words, or a short descriptive expression, such as:

- How to write SEO title
- How to find long-tail keywords
- How to determine search intent
- Recommended free SEO tools more suitable for novices

You will find that a good anchor text is not "flower", but "clear".

| To push old articles, hard link to irrelevant pages | It looks like an operational action, not like a content service |
| The same page is linked repeatedly in an article | It is not necessary, but it seems deliberate |

The internal link problem of many websites is not that they don’t work hard, but that they regard links as actions instead of paths. **

## If you want to organize the internal links in the site now, you can directly follow these 5 steps.

### Step one: First list the 5 to 10 most important core articles on your site
Don’t overhaul the entire site at once, focus on the main content first.

### Step 2: Write a sentence for each article: "What problem does this article solve?"
This step is particularly important. The questions are not clearly written, and the internal link relationships are often unclear.

### Step 3: Arrange the articles into links according to "what to read first and what to read later"
For example: choose words → judge intention → write title → write description → make layout → make internal links.

### Step 4: Go back to the text and only add links where additional explanation is really needed.
It doesn’t matter if it’s less, the key is smoothness.

### Step 5: Focus on checking the end and core paragraphs of the article
These two positions are usually the easiest to add natural internal links.

If you can complete these 5 steps first, the overall content of the site will be more obvious than simply posting a few more articles.

## My most common method now is to draw the content relationship first, and then let AI help me see where the link is too hard.

AI is now very suitable to help do one thing:

**Check whether your content relationship diagram is too cluttered. **

My own habit is to first list the topic, target keywords, and user stages of each article, and then let different models help me look at it:

- Which pages should actually link to each other?
- Which anchor text is too template-like?
- Where adding links will interrupt reading

If I want to quickly see the difference in judgment between different models, sometimes I will directly use **AI Comparison** and look at the recommendations of Claude, GPT, and DeepSeek at the same time. Not to let it make the decision for me, but to discover faster:

**Is this link helping users, or is it just satisfying my own impulse of "wanting to do SEO". **

This step may seem small, but it can especially reduce the problem of "a lot of technology is added, but it becomes increasingly awkward to read".

## Write at the end

Regarding SEO internal links, I feel more and more now that it is not as simple as “adding a few more links”.

It's more like answering a question:

**After reading this, where should the user go next? **

If you think about this issue clearly, the internal links will usually not be messy.

The content of many websites is becoming more and more scattered. It is not necessarily because there are not enough articles, nor is it necessarily because the keywords are not accurate enough. It is because each article only cares about itself and does not really connect the content of the entire website.

So if you happen to be organizing old articles recently, I highly recommend that you don’t rush to add a bunch of links.

First take out the content on the site, take a look at the relationship between them, and then decide which places should be connected and which places should not be hard-wired.

If you do the right internal linking, it will be easier for search engines to recognize what your website is about, and it will be easier for users to follow your content.

This is where it's really valuable.
