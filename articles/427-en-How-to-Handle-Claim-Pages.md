## Why do many cross-border e-commerce, independent websites, and DTC brand websites have abnormal international logistics and after-sales processes? In the end, what is most likely to be released is not the product page and help page, but a series of claim pages, claim application pages, and compensation progress pages?

This is actually quite common.

Especially when doing projects such as cross-border e-commerce, international distribution, overseas warehouses, and brand independent stations, as long as the station accepts lost items, damaged items, missing items, receipt disputes, transportation insurance, and after-sales compensation processes, it is easy for the system to slowly grow these addresses:

- `/claim`
- `/compensation`
- `/damage-claim`
- `/shipping-claim`
- `/claim/progress`
- `/after-sales/claim?orderNo=20260326027`
- `/support/compensation?waybill=SF1234567890`
- There are even pages such as compensation application page, claim result page, claim progress page, evidence upload page, carrier complaint page, and compensation payment account page.

Many teams saw these pages for the first time and had similar reactions:

- The claim page is also an official page, so there seems to be no problem with including it.
- Users will originally search for "How to compensate for lost international express delivery" and "How to claim for damaged parcels", so can these pages also receive traffic easily?
- The page has status, amount, and processing timeline, which looks more complete than many blank functional pages.
- The system generates it by default. Leave it for now and organize it later when you have time.

It doesn't sound entirely unreasonable.

But after you run the site for a while, you will often find that the claim page is particularly easy to slowly turn into the following situation:

- The page is highly dependent on specific orders, specific waybills, specific reasons for compensation, and specific processing progress.
- When users click in from the search results, they often first see "Please log in to view details", "This claim is being processed" or "This record has nothing to do with you"
- With too many parameters, it is easy to grow a bunch of approximate URLs
- The compensation rules page, claim explanation page, logistics exception FAQ, and insurance claims help page that should really handle search needs are not strengthened.
- What search engines capture is a bunch of process pages, result pages, and attachment pages, rather than public content pages that can stably answer questions.

So if you are also studying **How to handle the claim page** recently, I will give you a very straightforward judgment first:

**Most claims pages are essentially "after-sales process pages", "exception handling pages" and "compensation collaboration pages", not "public search landing pages". It is certainly important for customer service diversion, user comfort and internal fulfillment collaboration, but most of the time it is not suitable to be promoted as a key SEO page. What really needs to be done is not to simply release or block it across the board, but to distinguish first: whether this URL is serving a specific order or answering a public search question. **

## What problem does the claim page solve? It’s not just “the page that tells you whether you can compensate”

Many people’s understanding of the claim page is still limited to:

- A page to submit a compensation application after a lost item
- A page for supplementary materials after the package is damaged
- A very common results page or progress page in the after-sales process

This understanding is not wrong, but it is still a bit shallow.

A real claims page usually addresses at least three things.

### 1. Its core mission is to serve users who have placed orders, not to undertake open search needs

The core tasks of the claim page are generally:

- Tell the user whether the compensation application has been submitted successfully
- Tell the user whether they are currently under review, supplementary materials, carrier verification, or the compensation has been completed
- Tell the user whether to upload photos, reissue invoices, supplement receipt certificates, or wait for the platform to process
- Allow customer service, warehouses, carriers and users to form a unified judgment on the same exception
- Guide users from "How can I compensate for my package?" to "What should I do next?"

In other words, it mainly serves people who have already had a transactional relationship, rather than serving people who are still searching for answers to their questions.

### 2. It is usually deeply tied to order information, reasons for compensation, and evidence materials.

For example:

- Order number
- Waybill number
- Carrier name
- Reason for claim
-Whether to purchase transportation insurance
- Product amount or declared amount
- Damaged photos, receipt certificates, and chat records uploaded by users
- Is the current status "to be accepted", "to be supplemented", "under review", "compensation in progress" or "case closed"

This determines that the claim page is usually not a stable, unified page suitable for public indexing, but a type of page that changes continuously with the user's identity, claim information, carrier feedback and compensation progress.

### 3. It has business value, but it does not mean it has SEO value.

The claim page is of course important.

Without it, users have no idea what materials they should fill in, whether the platform will compensate, or when the money will arrive; customer service will also be questioned over and over again.

But it should be noted that ** business is very important, which does not mean that it is suitable to get traffic in natural search. **

When users search, they are usually more concerned about:

- How to claim for lost international express delivery
- What materials should be prepared if the package is damaged?
-How long does it usually take for a claim to be resolved?
- What is the difference between platform compensation and carrier compensation?
- Can I still make a claim without insurance?
- Can I still appeal after my claim is rejected?
- How long does it take for the compensation to arrive?

What is really more suitable to handle these search needs is usually not a private claims page, but:

- Claim description page
- Compensation rules page
- Logistics exception FAQ page
- Insurance claims help page
- After-sales policy page
- Lost/Damage Handling Guide Page

If these problems are finally handled by the claim page, it is most likely not that the structure is cleverly designed, but that the content layer, after-sales layer, and fulfillment layer are mixed together.

## How to deal with the claim page? I would suggest handling these 5 situations separately first.

### 1. If it is just a standard claim application page, compensation progress page, or claim result page, there is usually no need to treat it as a key SEO page.

This is the most common category.

For example:

- Claim application page
- Claim progress page
- Compensation results page
- Supplements page
- Page under review
- Compensation to account page
- Complaint handling center page

This type of page is of course important to old users, but the problem is also obvious:

**Most of them do not exist to meet open search needs. **

After users click in from the search results, the common feelings are often:

- This is not the description I'm looking for, just a specific claim record
- You can’t see the complete content without logging in
- Even if I can see it, it has nothing to do with my order.
- What I really want to know is "how to compensate", "what to compensate for" and "how long to compensate", not what stage of processing a certain order is now.

So a standard claims page is usually better suited as a process page rather than a focused SEO page.

In a word: **The claim page can be very important, but most of the time this importance is important in the sense of after-sales and compensation, not in the sense of natural search. **

### 2. If the real search value is "How to compensate for lost items", "How to claim for damage", "How long does it take to get results for claims", don't let the claim results page handle the traffic for them.

This misunderstanding is also very common.

Some teams will subconsciously feel:

- Users will search for compensation by nature, so there is no harm in including the claims page.
- There is an amount, a timeline, and maybe some long-tail words on the page.
- Users always have to go to the order page, so it doesn’t matter if they include the claim page.

But from an SEO perspective, this is often shaky.

Because users often search for something other than:

- Where is my claim record?
- What does a certain claim progress page look like?

Instead:

- After a piece is lost in logistics, should you contact the seller or the carrier?
- What photos and proof are needed after the package is damaged?
- What is the difference between platform compensation standards and insurance compensation standards?
-Why is claims review so slow?
- Can I continue to appeal if my claim is rejected?
- Where are compensation usually paid and how long does it take to arrive?

These questions are more appropriately asked by:

- Claim description page
- Claims FAQ page
- Compensation policy page
- Logistics exception help page
- Insurance claim explanation page
- After-sales help center

To take over.

**Don’t hide the content that should be disclosed publicly in the after-sales system, and then expect the claim results page to get search traffic on its own. **

### 3. If the system generates sub-paths and parameter URLs such as claim, compensation, damage, evidence, and appeal, you must first distinguish between "functional URL" and "index URL"

The real problem with many website claim pages is not that there is only one `/claim`, but that it also generates many variations:

- `/claim?orderNo=20260326027`
- `/compensation/progress?id=88301`
- `/damage-claim?carrier=ups`
- `/evidence/upload?case=claim`
- `/appeal/result?scene=email`
- `/claim/status?page=2`
- `/support/compensation?waybill=SF1234567890`
- `/after-sales/claim?reason=damaged`

These addresses may certainly be useful to the process.

But for SEO, they often mean:

- A large number of near-duplicate URLs
- Parameters break up page signals
- The crawled resources are slowly eaten up by a bunch of after-sales process pages
- Emails, text messages, and site notifications can easily expose various temporary addresses.
- Some pages even bring out sensitive context such as order numbers, compensation amounts, evidence pictures, and user privacy.

So when processing the claim page, a very practical action is:

- First distinguish what is the URL that users must use to process claims
- Distinguish what public pages search engines really should see
- Parametric addresses should not be output everywhere by default
- The entrances in emails, text messages, and help centers should be as unified as possible

**The things that are most likely to get out of control on a claim page are often not the text, but the status, parameters, attachments and derived sub-paths. **

### 4. If you have both a "public claim description page" and a "private claim details page", be sure to completely separate these two types of pages.

This is particularly easy to overlook.

Many cross-border websites actually have two sets of pages at the same time:

- One set is a publicly accessible claim explanation page, compensation FAQ, logistics exception help page, and insurance claims rules page
- One set is the claim page, compensation details page, supplement page, and results page that can only be viewed after logging in or with an order voucher.

Some teams will mix these two types of things, and several problems will arise as a result:

- The help page is thinly written and the real information is buried in the claim details
- The private claim page is caught by search engines, but the public explanation page is not strong
- The user searched for "How to compensate for damaged parcels" and "How to claim for lost international express delivery", but the landing page turned into a results page that required login.

A more stable approach is usually:

- Use public help pages to answer common questions
- Use the description page to accept rule-type, material-type and time-sensitive keywords
- Use the claim page to only serve specific users and specific orders
- If there is indeed a function for tourists to check the claim status, try not to let the query result page participate in the index by default.

In a word: **The public description page is responsible for answering questions, and the private claim details page is responsible for processing specific orders. Do not mix these two types of pages. **

### 5. If you do not plan to let the claim page participate in ranking, unify noindex, login interception, sitemap, canonical, attachment rules and internal links together

Many claims page SEO problems are not because of "is there such a page", but because the signals are fighting each other.

The most common situations are:

- The team says the claim page is not important
- As a result, claim, compensation, and appeal related URLs are still being submitted in the sitemap.
- The evidence upload page, supplement page, and results page can also be brought up repeatedly by the help center, order center, and site search.
- Output parameter addresses everywhere in email and SMS templates
- Canonical, noindex, login interception, and parameter processing rules are not consistent with each other.
- Attached pictures, receipt certificates, and compensation screenshots were also exposed.

If you decide not to focus on SEO on your claim page, a more stable idea is usually:

- The core claims process page and results page are located in the sitemap
- Private claim details pages should have login protection or clear indexing policy if possible
- Do not expose yourself publicly on the evidence upload page, attachment page and export page
- Don’t package the claim page into a content page for internal links
- Really searchable description pages, rules pages and FAQ, separate public pages

Don’t fight about technical signals. This is more important than arguing about whether to include the claim page or not.

## The 4 most common SEO mistakes I see on claim pages are more likely to mess up the website than not doing them.

### 1. It is believed that “users will search for compensation”, so the claim details page is naturally suitable for ranking.

Some people will indeed search for:

- International express claims
- Claims for damaged packages
- Compensation standards for lost items
- Brand name + claim settlement

But the core requirement here is usually not to look at a certain claim record, but to find:

- How to compensate
- Who will pay?
-How much to pay
- How long does it take to get results?
- What materials are needed
- What else can you do after being rejected?

What are really suitable for undertaking such words are often brand help pages, compensation rules pages, and logistics exception FAQs, rather than a specific claim record.

### 2. The claim page, supplement page, result page, and evidence upload page were released in batches by the system. As a result, a bunch of thin pages appeared on the entire site.

This problem is also very common.

You thought it was just a claims module, but what was actually released was a whole string:

- Claim application page
- Supplements page
- Progress page
- Results page
- Attachment upload page
- Compensation to account page
- Email notification jump page

If these pages can be crawled, entered into the sitemap, and brought out by internal links, then in the end there will be a large number of process pages on the site that have little independent search value.

### 3. Hide the compensation standards, material requirements, and processing timeliness that are truly searchable in the post-login page.

This is the biggest disadvantage of many websites.

The questions that users actually search for are clearly very clear:

- What materials are needed to make a claim?
- How to calculate the compensation standard for lost items?
- What photos need to be taken for damage claims?
- Can I be compensated if I didn’t buy insurance?
-Why is claims review so slow?
- How to appeal after a claim fails?

There is no independent explanation page in the results site. All answers must be logged in, placed, and then clicked on the claim page to see all answers.

The consequences of doing this are usually:

-Search engines don’t see the complete answer
- New users are also unable to access key information
- Customer service pressure increases
- The claim page is forced to perform search tasks it should not be undertaking

### 4. He said he didn’t want to do claim page SEO, but in the end, sitemap, templates, attachment rules, and parameter rules all did their own thing.

This type of problem is extremely common.

For example:

- The claim page does not want to be included, but the sitemap is still being submitted.
- I wanted to keep a low profile, but emails and text messages made all the claim parameter URLs public.
- I want to reduce search engine crawling, but the help page, order center, and site search are still giving entry points repeatedly.
- Want to unify signals, but noindex, canonical, login interception, and attachment permissions are inconsistent with each other
- Even compensation screenshots, receipt certificates, and invoice attachments can be accessed externally

It’s not that the claim page itself is complex, it’s that the signals you’re giving it are too conflicting.

## If you want to check your website's claims page settings now, I recommend going through them in this order

### Step 1: List all claim-related URL types first

At least sort out the following:

- Claim application page
- Compensation progress page
- Supplements page
- Results page
- Appeal page
- Attachment upload page
- Parametric variant URL
- Screenshots, attachments and export URLs

Many problems are not that you don’t know how to deal with them, but that you simply haven’t read all the claims-related pages.

### Step 2: Determine whether these pages are serving "specific orders" or answering "open questions"

A very simple way to judge is:

- If the page content is strongly dependent on a certain user, a certain order, or a certain claim result, it will most likely be more biased toward the process page.
- If the content of the page can stably answer questions that a large number of users may search, it will be more like a public landing page.

Don't lump these two types of tasks into one.

### Step 3: Separate the questions that really have search value and create separate public pages

For example:

- Statement of claim
- Compensation rules FAQ
- Material preparation guide
- Insurance claim explanation page
- Help page for lost/damaged items
- After-sales complaint instructions page

These contents alone are often more stable than letting the claim page carry SEO.

### Step 4: Unify technical signals and don’t let the after-sales compensation module grow a forest of crawlable claim pages.

Key points to check:

- Whether the sitemap is still submitting claim, compensation, appeal type URLs
- Whether the attachment page and parameter page are exposed everywhere
- Whether private pages have a clear indexing strategy
- Whether the public description page and the private claim page compete with each other?
- Are the links in emails, SMS templates, and help centers consistent?

### Step 5: When observing the results, don’t just look at “whether the claim page has been included”

What’s more important to look at is:

- Whether the claim explanation page, compensation FAQ, and after-sales policy page receive more concentrated traffic
- Are there a lot of meaningless process pages missing from the search results?
- When users search for compensation issues, can they land on the correct page faster?
-Whether customer service and after-sales inquiries reduce repeated problems due to clearer public explanations

Whether the claim page is really well handled does not depend on how many words it grabs, but whether it stays where it should.

## Should the claim page be included? Don’t ask about the “unified answer” first, ask first whether it is accepting public search requests.

**Should the claim page be included? **

My view has always been straightforward:

- If it is essentially a private after-sales process page, don’t treat it as a key SEO page
- If it is just a claim page, a compensation progress page, a supplement page, and a results page, then in most cases there is no need to feature it for inclusion.
- If the real search value is compensation standards, material requirements, time limits and appeal rules, make these contents a separate public page
-Only in rare cases, such as if you are making a public logistics help center, a public after-sales knowledge base, or a public claims rule center, it will be more like a page suitable for long-term searches.

So if you are still struggling with **how to handle the claim page**, here is my advice:

**First treat the claim page as an "after-sales process page" and a "compensation processing page", and then decide whether it should be included in the index; don't just put the claim application page, compensation progress page, results page and a bunch of claim parameter URLs into the search results just because the system generates them by default. **
