## Why is it that when many websites become e-commerce, independent websites or membership malls, what is most likely to be released in the end is not product pages and help pages, but a series of delivery failure pages, delivery failure result pages and re-delivery status pages?

This is actually more common than many people think.

Especially when working on projects such as e-commerce, DTC brand sites, cross-border independent sites, and local distribution malls, as long as there is a logistics fulfillment process in the site, it is easy for the system to slowly grow these addresses:

- `/delivery-failed`
- `/failed-delivery`
- `/attempt-failed?orderNo=20260325001`
- `/redelivery`
- `/delivery-exception`
- `/track?status=failed`
- `/shipment/93827/failed`
- There are even status pages such as delivery failure result page, recipient cannot be contacted page, address exception page, re-delivery page, and self-pickup transfer page.

When many teams see these pages, their first reaction is usually:

- The delivery failure page is also an official page, so there seems to be no problem in keeping it for inclusion.
- Users will originally search for "What to do if express delivery fails" and "What to do if delivery fails", so can these pages also receive traffic easily?
- The page has status, time and reason, which looks more complete than many blank function pages.
- The system generates it by default. Don’t leave it alone for now and organize it later.

None of it sounds completely unreasonable.

But after you do it for a while, you will often find that the delivery failure page is particularly easy to gradually become like this:

- The page is highly dependent on the login status. If you are not logged in, you will not be able to see the complete information.
- Try to strongly bind the page to specific orders, specific packages, and specific deliveries.
- With too many parameters and states, it is easy to grow a bunch of approximate URLs
- When users click in from the search results, they often first see "Please log in first to view", "Please enter the order number" or a failure record that has nothing to do with them.
- The delivery exception explanation page, re-delivery help page, and signed-but-not-received FAQ that really should handle the search needs are not strengthened.

So if you are also studying **how to deal with the delivery failure page** recently, I will give you a very straightforward judgment first:

**Most delivery failure pages are essentially "fulfillment exception handling pages" and "order service pages", not "public search landing pages". It is of course important for after-sales judgment, customer service diversion and user experience, but most of the time it is not suitable to be promoted as a key SEO page. What really needs to be done is not to simply release or block it across the board, but to distinguish first: whether this URL is serving users who have already placed orders, or whether it is answering public search questions. **

## What problem does the delivery failure page solve? It's not just "Show the page that wasn't delivered"

Many people still only understand the delivery failure page as follows:

- If the express delivery is not delivered, the status will be displayed later.
- A place where users can check the reasons for failure and re-delivery entrance
- A very ordinary results page in the logistics process

This understanding is not wrong, but it is still a bit shallow.

Real delivery failure pages usually address at least three things.

### 1. Its core mission is to serve users who have placed orders, not to undertake open search needs

The core tasks of the delivery failure page are generally:

- Tell the user why the delivery was not successful this time
- Tell users what they can do next, such as changing address, changing time, contacting carrier, transferring to self-pickup
- Let customer service, after-sales and users form a unified judgment on abnormal status
- Record the results of a specific delivery attempt
- Guide users to the next action instead of getting stuck in a state

In other words, it mainly serves people who have already established transactional relationships, rather than serving people who are still searching and comparing.

### 2. It is usually deeply tied to order information, address information, and delivery reasons.

for example:

- Order number
- Waybill number
- Delivery time
- Reason for failure
- Address fragment
- Contact records
-Re-delivery time
- Pick up point information

This determines that the delivery failure page is usually not a stable, unified page suitable for public indexing, but a type of page that changes continuously with the user's identity, logistics status and exception handling progress.

### 3. It has business value, but it does not mean it has SEO value.

The delivery failure page is of course important.

Without it, users would be anxious, customer service would be bombarded with questions, and after-sales judgment would be even more confusing.

But it should be noted that ** business is very important, which does not mean that it is suitable to get traffic in natural search. **

When users search, they are usually more concerned about:

- What to do if express delivery fails
- How to re-deliver if the recipient cannot be contacted?
- Can I still change my address if it is wrong?
- Shows how long it will take before delivery fails
- How to pick up the parcel after transferring it to self-pickup?
- Will it be returned to the warehouse if delivery fails?
- What to do if international parcel delivery fails?

What is truly more suitable to handle these search needs is usually not a private delivery failure results page, but:

- Delivery exception explanation page
- Redistribute help page
- Address modification rule page
- Pickup instructions page
- Logistics exception FAQ
- After-sales help center articles

If these problems are ultimately solved by the delivery failure page, it is most likely not that the structure is cleverly designed, but that the content layer and fulfillment layer are mixed together.

## How to deal with the delivery failure page? I would suggest handling these 5 situations separately first.

### 1. If it is just a standard delivery failure result page, failure reason page, and re-delivery status page, there is usually no need to treat it as a key SEO page.

This is the most common category.

for example:

- Delivery failure result page
- Delivery failure details page
- Recipient page cannot be contacted
- Address exception page
- Resend the processing page
- Redirected to self-pickup point results page
- Failure record page

This type of page is of course important to old users, but the problem is also obvious:

**Most of them do not exist to meet open search needs. **

After users click in from the search results, the common feelings are often:

- This is not the description I am looking for, just an abnormal record
- You can’t see the content without logging in
- Even if you can see it, it has nothing to do with my package.
- What I really want to know is the processing method, rules and timeliness, not a specific failure status

Therefore, the standard delivery failure page is usually more suitable as a process page rather than a focus SEO page.

In a word: **The delivery failure page can be very important, but this importance is mostly important in the sense of fulfillment exception handling, not in the sense of natural search. **

### 2. If the real search value is "why delivery failed", "how to re-deliver" and "how to transfer to self-pickup", don't let the failure results page take over the traffic for them.

This misunderstanding is also very common.

Some teams will subconsciously feel:

- Users will naturally search for delivery failures, so there is no harm in including failure pages.
- The page has failure reasons and operation entry points, and maybe some long-tail words.
- Users always have to go to the order or logistics page, so it doesn’t matter if they include the failure page.

But from an SEO perspective, this is often shaky.

Because users often search for something other than:

- Where is my delivery failure details page?
- What does the delivery failure result page look like?

Instead:

- How to re-deliver after the recipient cannot be contacted?
- I wrote the wrong address, can I change it?
- How long will it take for another delivery after a delivery failure?
- What to do after the package is transferred to the pick-up point?
- Will there be refunds if multiple delivery failures occur?
- Who to contact if international logistics delivery fails?

These questions are more appropriately asked by:

- Delivery exception explanation page
- Redistribute help page
- Address modification rule page
- Pick up FAQ page
- Return to warehouse instructions page
- International logistics exception help page

To take over.

**Don’t hide the content that should be disclosed publicly in the failure status system, and then expect to get search traffic by sending the failure results page. **

### 3. If the system generates sub-paths and parameter URLs such as failed, attempt, reason, carrier, redelivery, and pickup, you must first distinguish between "functional URL" and "index URL"

The real problem with the delivery failure pages of many websites is not that there is only one `/delivery-failed`, but that it also generates many variations:

- `/attempt-failed?orderNo=20260325001`
- `/delivery-failed?reason=address`
- `/redelivery?carrier=sf`
- `/track?status=failed`
- `/failed-delivery?scene=sms`
- `/shipment/93827/failed`
- `/pickup-transfer?id=88271`
- `/delivery-exception?page=2`

These addresses may certainly be useful in the fulfillment process.

But for SEO, they often mean:

- A large number of near-duplicate URLs
- Parameters break up page signals
- The crawled resources are slowly eaten by the abnormal status page
- Emails, text messages, and site notifications can easily expose a bunch of temporary addresses.
- Some pages even bring out sensitive context such as order numbers, address fragments, and carrier notes.

So when dealing with delivery failure pages, a very practical action is:

- First distinguish what is the URL that users must use to view abnormal status
- Distinguish what public pages search engines really should see
- Parametric addresses are output everywhere by default
- The entrances in order notifications, shipping text messages, and help documents should be as unified as possible

**The most likely thing on a delivery failure page to get out of control is not the text, but the status, parameters and derived sub-paths. **

### 4. If you have both a "public delivery exception description page" and a "private delivery failure result page", be sure to completely separate these two types of pages.

This is particularly easy to overlook.

Many websites actually have two sets of pages at the same time:

- One set is a publicly accessible delivery exception explanation page, re-delivery help page, self-pickup explanation page, and logistics FAQ page
- One set is the delivery failure result page, failure details page, and re-delivery progress page that can only be viewed after logging in or with order credentials.

Some teams will mix these two types of things, and several problems will arise as a result:

- The help page is very thin, and the real information is buried in the failure details.
- The private failure page is caught by search engines, but the public explanation page is not powerful
- Users search for "What to do if express delivery fails" and "What to do if the recipient cannot be contacted", but the landing page turns into a status page that requires login.

A more stable approach is usually:

- Use public help pages to answer common questions
- Use description pages to accept time-sensitive, rule-based and exception-handling keywords
- Use the delivery failure result page to only serve specific users and specific packages
- If there is indeed a function for visitors to query abnormal status, try not to let the query result page participate in the index by default.

In a word: **The public explanation page is responsible for answering questions, and the private failure page is responsible for handling specific packages. Do not mix these two types of pages. **

### 5. If you do not intend to allow delivery failure pages to participate in rankings, unify noindex, login interception, sitemap, canonical, internal links and parameter rules together

Many SEO problems with failed delivery pages are not caused by "whether there is such a page", but because the signals are fighting with each other.

The most common situations are:

- The team says the failure page is not important
- As a result, URLs related to failed-delivery, redelivery, and delivery-exception are still being submitted in the sitemap.
- Output parameter addresses everywhere in email and SMS templates
- Some failure pages can also be brought up repeatedly by the help center, order center, and site search
- Canonical, noindex, and parameter processing rules are not consistent with each other.

If you decide to send failure pages without focusing on SEO, a more stable idea is usually:

- The core failure page and result page are not included in the sitemap
- Private failure details page should have login protection or clear indexing strategy as much as possible
- Don’t expose parameter-type exception URL everywhere
- Don’t wrap the failure page into a content page for internal links
- Delivery exception descriptions, re-delivery help, self-pickup rules that are truly searchable, and have a separate public page

Don’t fight about technical signals. This is more important than arguing about whether to include failed delivery pages or not.

## The 4 most common SEO mistakes I see for failed delivery pages are more likely to mess up the site than not doing them.

### 1. It is believed that "users will search for delivery failure", so the failure details page is naturally suitable for participating in rankings.

Some people will indeed search for:

- Brand name + delivery failed
- Brand name + delivery failure
- Brand Name + Resend
- Brand name + express delivery not delivered

But the core requirement here is usually not to look at a certain failure record, but to find:

- Solutions
- Re-delivery entrance
- Contact channels
- aging instructions
- Manual support paths

The ones that are really suitable for such words are often brand help pages, delivery exception explanation pages, and re-delivery FAQs, rather than a specific failure record.

### 2. The delivery failure page, signature page, logistics exception page, and re-delivery page were released in batches by the system. As a result, a bunch of thin pages appeared on the entire site.

This problem is common in e-commerce, cross-border websites, and brand independent websites.

You thought it was just an exception status module, but what was actually released was a whole string:

- Delivery failure result page
- Failure details page
- Resend page
- Self-pickup transfer page
- Logistics exception page
- Address exception page
- Failure record page
- Process results page

If these pages can be crawled, entered into the sitemap, and taken out by internal jumps, then in the end there will be a large number of process pages in the site that have little independent search value.

### 3. Hide exception descriptions, reassignment rules, and self-pickup processes that are truly searchable in the post-login page.

This is the biggest disadvantage of many websites.

The questions that users actually search for are clearly very clear:

- How long does it take to deliver again after delivery failure?
- What to do if the recipient cannot be contacted?
- Will delivery fail if the address is not detailed?
- How to pick up items after changing to self-pickup?
- Will multiple delivery failures be returned to the warehouse?
- How to appeal if overseas parcel delivery fails?

There is no independent explanation page in the results site. All answers can only be found by logging in, placing an order, and then clicking on the failure page.

The consequences of doing this are usually:

-Search engines don’t see the complete answer
- New users are also unable to access key information
- Customer service pressure increases
- The delivery failure page is forced to undertake search tasks that it should not undertake.

### 4. He said he didn’t want to do SEO for delivery failure pages, but in the end, sitemap, internal links, templates, and parameter rules all did their own thing.

This type of problem is extremely common.

for example:

- The delivery failed page does not want to be included, but the sitemap is still being submitted.
- I wanted to keep a low profile, but text messages and emails disclosed all the failed parameter URLs.
- I want to reduce search engine crawling, but the help page, order center, and site search are still giving entry points repeatedly.
- Want to unify signals, but canonical, login interception, status code, and parameter rules are inconsistent with each other

It’s not that the delivery failure page itself is complicated, it’s that the signals you give it are too contradictory.

## If you want to check the website's delivery failure page settings now, I recommend going through it in this order.

### Step one: First list all URL types related to delivery failure.

At least sort out the following:

- Delivery failure result page
- Failure details page
- Resend page
- Self-pickup transfer page
- Address exception page
- Logistics exception page
- Process results page
- Parametric variant URL

Many problems are not that you don’t know how to deal with them, but that you simply don’t understand all the pages related to delivery failure.

### Step 2: Determine whether these pages are serving "specific packages" or answering "open questions"

A very simple way to judge is:

- If the page content is strongly dependent on a certain user, a certain order, or a certain delivery failure reason, it will most likely be more biased towards the process page.
- If the content of the page can stably answer questions that a large number of users may search, it will be more like a public landing page.

Don't lump these two types of tasks into one.

### Step 3: Separate the questions that really have search value and create separate public pages

for example:

-Re-delivery instructions
- What to do if the recipient cannot be contacted
-Address modification rules
- Instructions for picking up items at self-pickup points
- Return rules after multiple delivery failures
- International Logistics Delivery Abnormal FAQ

Creating these contents alone is often more stable than letting the delivery failure page carry SEO.

### Step 4: Unify technical signals and don’t let the abnormal fulfillment module grow a forest of crawlable status pages.

Key points to check:

- Is the sitemap still submitting failed, attempt, redelivery, delivery-exception URLs?
- Whether the parameter page is exposed everywhere
- Whether private pages have a clear indexing strategy
- Whether the public explanation page and the private failure page compete for each other's words?
- Are the links in text messages, email templates, and help centers consistent?

### Step 5: When observing the results, don’t just look at “whether the delivery failure page has been included”

What’s more important to look at is:

- Whether the delivery exception explanation page, help page, and FAQ page receive more concentrated traffic
- Are there a lot of meaningless process pages missing from the search results?
- When users search for delivery failure issues, can they land on the correct page faster?
-Whether customer service and after-sales inquiries reduce repeated problems due to clearer public explanations

Whether the delivery failure page is handled well or not depends not on how many words it grabs, but on whether it stays where it should.

## Should the delivery failure page be included? Don’t ask about the “unified answer” first, ask first whether it is accepting public search requests.

**Should the delivery failure page be included? **

My view has always been straightforward:

- If it is essentially a private fulfillment exception handling page, don’t treat it as a key SEO page
- If it is just the failure status, failure reason, resend result, and self-retrieval transfer result, then in most cases there is no need to promote the collection.
- If what is really valuable for searching is exception descriptions, reassignment rules, address modifications, and self-help, then make these contents a separate public page.
-Only in rare cases, such as when you make a public delivery exception help center, a public re-delivery instruction center, or a public delivery failure FAQ center, it is more like a page suitable for long-term searches.

So if you are still struggling with **how to deal with the delivery failure page**, here is my suggestion:

**First treat the delivery failure page as a "fulfillment exception service page", and then decide whether it should be included in the index; don't just put the failure details page, re-delivery page, self-pickup transfer page and a bunch of failed parameter URLs into the search results just because the system generates them by default. **
