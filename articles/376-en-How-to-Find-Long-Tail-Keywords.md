# How to find long tail keywords? Don’t just focus on the big words, these 5 search methods make it easier to find words that can be written and ranked

## Why do many people get stuck in "big word anxiety" when they do keyword research?

Many people just start doing SEO, and the easiest words to focus on are the words that are very "valuable" at first glance.

For example:

- AI tools
- SEO optimization
- Content marketing
- ChatGPT
- Keyword Tool

The words certainly look good.

The search volume is large, and the name is also large. It seems that as long as you are ranked high, the traffic will come in a rush.

But the reality is usually: **You are just about to write, and there are already a bunch of old sites, big sites, tool sites, encyclopedia sites, and forum posts lined up in front. **

This is where the problem comes.

It’s not that you can’t write, or that you don’t write carefully enough, but that the goal you set your sights on at the beginning may not be suitable for your current stage.

Especially for new websites, small websites, and websites with just starting content, they really need to start with a few very large words, and the difficulty is usually much higher than many people think.

So when I do keyword research now, I will not grab the most conspicuous big words first, but prefer to look for **long tail keywords** first.

The reason is simple:

**Long tail words are more specific, competition is often lower, and user needs are clearer. It is easier to match search intent when written. **

To put it bluntly, big words are like competing for position on the road; long-tail words are more like looking for people who have come with clear questions.

This group of people may not necessarily be the largest, but they are usually easier to click on, easier to read, and easier to convert.

---

## Long tail keywords are not as simple as "longer words"

When many people hear "long tail keywords", their first reaction is:

“Is it a keyword with more words?”

This understanding cannot be wrong, but it is not enough.

The really important thing about long-tail keywords is not that they have a few words, but that they are more specific, closer to real problems, and closer to what users want to solve at the moment.

For example:

- **Big word**: SEO tools
- **Long tail words**: Free SEO tool recommendations
- **Longer long tail words**: What are the free SEO tools suitable for novices?
- **Long tail words with scenarios**: What free SEO tools can be used in the early stage of an independent station?

You will find that the further the words go, the clearer the needs in the user's mind.

This is especially important for content.

Because you are not guessing "what the user probably wants to see", but you are answering a question that has been explained clearly.

This kind of words will be written more smoothly, the title will be better, the structure will be easier to create, and the conversion point will be more natural.

So don’t interpret long-tail keywords as “longer words.”

To be more precise, it is actually:

**More specific expression of needs. **

## I usually look at these 4 things before deciding whether this long-tail word is worth doing.

Not all long-tail words are worth writing.

Some words are long, but no one searches for them; some words are specific, but their intentions are confusing; and some words look like opportunities, but the top rankings are all giant pages.

So before I choose words, I usually go through these 4 questions:

### 1. Is the need behind this word clear enough?
For example, "AI tools" is too broad, but "free AI tools without registration" is much clearer.

### 2. Do people who search for this word want to understand, compare, or prepare to take action?
The intentions are different, and the content forms are also different. Don’t just write the tutorial words as recommendations, and don’t just talk about the concepts in the decision-making words.

### 3. Now in the search results page, do I also have the opportunity to do the content ranked first?
If the top 10 are all authoritative official websites, encyclopedias, and top tool sites, then don’t get too ahead of yourself.

### 4. Can this word easily bring up a set of related content?
Good words usually don’t stand alone. It can be extended into a series of topic choices, such as:
- Free SEO tool recommendations
- Are free SEO tools suitable for beginners?
- What is the difference between free SEO tools and paid tools?
- Which SEO tools should be used in the early stage of a new website?

If a word can naturally bring out a string of content, the value is usually higher.

## Method 1: First search from the search box drop-down and related searches. Don’t think it’s trivial. This step is often the most useful.

Many people look for keywords and open tools when they come up.

Of course no problem.

But if you have a limited budget, or just want to quickly find a batch of long-tail words that you can write about first, **Search box drop-down words** and **Related searches at the bottom of the search results page** are really useful.

Because these things are not made up by your brain, but are actually searched by users.

For example, if you search for "long tail keywords", if you scroll down, the following will usually appear:

- How to find long-tail keywords
- Long tail keyword tool
-What does long tail keyword mean?
- Long tail keyword mining method
- The difference between long-tail keywords and short-tail keywords

Looking further down the related searches will bring up more question-type expressions.

This word has two benefits:

1. **Closer to real search language**
2. **Especially suitable for titles and subsection titles**

When I am working on content myself, I often choose the first batch of topics from this step.

No need to pursue finding 100 of them at once.

Start by looking for 10 to 20 words that "look like real questions" and that's enough.

## Method 2: Look at the titles of competing products and similar content, but don’t just copy the titles, look at what kind of traffic it is eating.

This trick is very common, but many people use it too lightly.

They just copy the title of someone else’s article and then rephrase it.

This is not called keyword research, this is called repeating with others.

A more useful view is actually:

**Why is this title used for this content? What kind of search is it trying to catch? **

For example, if you see someone else writing:

- How to write SEO title
- How to write SEO description
- How to determine search intent
- How to find long-tail keywords

On the surface, they are just a few article titles.

But looking inside, you can actually find a very clear content link:

- First determine what the user is searching for
- Decide what to write
- Then decide how to start the title
- Then decide how to layout the keywords

At this time, what you get is not just "a word", but a series of topic selections.

I usually focus on the following:

- What question words appear repeatedly in the other party’s title?
- Is there a set of frequently asked questions in the subtitle?
- What are you still asking in the comment area and FAQ?
- Does the same website continue to write the same type of long-tail words?

This is much more useful than simply copying the title.

## Method 3: Go to the comment area, Q&A area, and community posts to look for "human words". Many long-tail words are not in the tool at all.

Many keyword tools will give you a bunch of words.

But there is a problem: ** tools are good at giving you "words like keywords", but not necessarily good at giving you "words like people would really ask". **

The places that can really bring good content inspiration are often found in these places:

- Zhihu question
- Xiaohongshu comment area
- Reddit post title
- V2EX post title
- Bilibili video comments
- Douyin/video account comment area
- E-commerce comment area

For example, the tool may only tell you:

- Recommended AI writing tools
- Comparison of AI writing tools

But the real expression in the comment area may be:

- How can things written by AI be changed so that they don’t look like AI?
- What should the boss do if he sees at a glance that it was written by AI?
- How to remedy the lack of copywriting in ChatGPT
- Which AI is more suitable for editing long articles, other than the one that only writes the beginning?

You see, these are the real questions.

And this kind of question, once sorted out, is a very typical source of long-tail words and topic selection.

A lot of content that can be typed is not fished out from the database, but heard from users' complaints.

## Method 4: Look through your own site data, customer service conversations and product scenarios to find long-tail words that are most relevant to your business.

This one is particularly easy to overlook.

Many people do keyword research and only look at external tools instead of the data they have.

In fact, if you already have a website, products, customer service records, reviews and feedback, and site search data, there are many more valuable words hidden in them.

For example:

- What users have searched for on the site
-What are the most common questions asked by customer service?
- What are most frequently mentioned in product reviews?
- Which step does the user get stuck at after trying it out?
- What search terms do article traffic come from?

The value of these words is often higher than the big industry words that "look professional".

Because they are closer to real business.

For example, if you make AI tool products, external tools will only give you “AI tool recommendations”;
But what your users may actually be searching for is:

- How to ask multiple AIs at the same time
- Can ChatGPT and Claude be used together?
- What should I do if I want to see multiple AI answers to a question?
- I don’t want to switch back and forth to the AI webpage. Is there a more trouble-free way?

The search volume for such words is not necessarily exaggerated, but is particularly relevant to products.

If you can catch such words, the traffic that comes will tend to be more accurate.

## Method 5: Use AI to help you expand words, but don’t let AI directly make decisions for you

AI is now very suitable for keyword divergence.

Especially when you already have a core word, a target user and a usage scenario, letting AI help you expand will be much faster than trying to figure it out alone.

But here is a premise:

**AI is suitable for diffusion, but not suitable for directly replacing judgment. **

What I usually do now is to feed it the following things first:

- Core themes
- Target readers
- Article purpose
- Known question words
- Scenes you wish to cover

Then have it give me respectively:

- Question-type long-tail words
- Contrastive long-tail words
- Decision-making long-tail words
- Scenario-type long-tail words

If you want to see it from different angles, I will also use **AI comparison** to throw the same word expansion requirement to different models and look at them together. Not to copy directly, but to see:

- Which words look too much like tool-generated words?
- Which statements are more likely to be searched by real users?
- Which angles I didn’t think of just now?

What really needs to be done in the end is not "Which model gives the most", but "Which word is more likely to be searched by users, and I can really write it".

## I usually delete these 4 things that “look like long tail words” first.

The more long-tail words, the better.

When I filter words myself, I usually delete the following first:

| FAQ | Why I exclude first |
|------|------|
| Just add infinite modifiers to the core word | It looks longer, but the intention is not clearer |
| The search expression is too awkward | It’s more like tool splicing than real people who can search |
| At first glance, it is not a word that I can do in the front row | Competition and page type do not match |
| It has nothing to do with business, readers, or conversions | Traffic may not be valuable even if it comes |

Words like this:

- Recommended collection of the latest, most complete and best free long-tail keyword tools in 2026

It's really long.

But if you look carefully, you will find that most of them are just for fun, not to express real needs.

The long-tail keywords that are truly worthy of development are usually not “more beautiful” but “more accurate”.

## If you want to start looking for words now, you can just follow this sequence.

This is a simplified process that I commonly use now.

### Step 1: Decide on a core theme first
For example: SEO title, SEO description, long-tail keywords, AI tool comparison.

### Step 2: Remove 4 types of questions
- what is
- how to do it
- Compare with whom
- Suitable for whom/what scene

### Step 3: Go to 4 places to get one round each
- Search box drop down
- Related searches
- Competitive product title/FAQ
- Comment area/community questions

### Step 4: Group words according to intent
- Informational
- Contrast type
- Decision-making
- Transaction/Action

### Step 5: Delete words that are not human-like, cannot be written, and have nothing to do with business
This step is particularly important.

It’s not that many people can’t find the words, but they are reluctant to delete them.

### Step 6: Pick 5 to 10 of the most specific and convenient ones to start writing.
Don’t try to finish all the words at once.

It’s more useful to write the first batch of content first than to hoard 300 words in a table.

---

## Write at the end

Regarding long-tail keywords, I feel more and more now that it is not essentially "looking for longer words", but "looking for more specific questions".

The more specific the question, the clearer the user, and the easier it is to write the content to the point.

Especially for new sites, small teams, and individual webmasters, if you always focus on the hottest big words in the early stage, it is really easy to start doubting life as you write.

On the other hand, if you are willing to start with long-tail words first, many things will go much smoother:

- Titles are easier to come up with
- Articles are easier to focus on
- Ranking makes it easier to get the first breakthroughs
- After users come in, it is easier for them to think, “Yes, this article is what I am looking for.”

So if you happen to be planning to do keyword research today, I suggest you don’t open a bunch of complicated tools first.

First, search for the core words you want to work on, and carefully search for drop-down words, related searches, questions in the comment area, and competitive product titles.

You will probably find that the long-tail words that are really worth writing are not as far-reaching as you thought.

They are hidden in the words most easily spoken by users.
