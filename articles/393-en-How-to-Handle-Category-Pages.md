## Why do many websites have more and more category pages, but in the end few of them can really get traffic?

This is very common in content sites, e-commerce sites, tool sites, and SaaS sites.

When the website first started, everyone’s idea of ​​creating categories was quite normal:

- Make it easier for users to find content
- Categorize products and articles into categories
- Make the site structure look clearer
- Make a few more SEO entrances easily

As a result, when there are too many categories, problems arise.

You can easily see this happening:

- `/category/seo`
- `/category/ai-tools`
- `/category/content-marketing`
- `/category/growth`
- `/category/news`
- `/category/case-study`

The front desk seems to have quite complete columns, and the back desk also feels that the structure is quite complete.

But when it comes to search traffic, the performance of many category pages is embarrassing:

- Some categories have too many themes and cannot meet the search needs at all.
- Some categories have too little content, and there are only three or five articles when you open it.
- Some categories, tag pages, topic pages, and search pages are competing for the same batch of words.
- Some category pages have almost no information except titles and lists.

Eventually it will become a very common situation:

**There are a lot of category pages, but only a few of them can really steadily attract traffic, clicks, and inclusions. **

Many teams tend to go to two extremes when dealing with category pages:

- Either do them all, I think "the more categories, the more entrances"
- Or don’t do it at all, thinking “Isn’t the category page just an article list page?”

To be honest, both of these are not stable.

If you do it all, it will be easy to turn the website into a bunch of aggregated pages with blurred boundaries;
If you don’t do it at all, you will waste the pages that are very suitable for carrying keywords and navigation portals.

So if you are also studying **how to deal with category pages** recently, let me give you a direct conclusion:

**Category pages do not naturally have SEO value, nor do they naturally have no value. The key is not "whether it is a category page", but whether this category has a clear theme, stable content, independent needs, and whether it is serving real users, or is it just a column conveniently separated by the backend for management convenience. **

## What problem is the category page solving? It’s not just about “dividing columns into sections for webmasters”

When many people mention category pages, their first reaction is:

- List page
- Aggregation page
- Template page
- easy to repeat
- Not necessarily content

These concerns are correct.

But you have to admit that the category page itself is not useless.

It's doing at least three things:

### 1. Help users quickly narrow down their browsing scope

When a website contains more and more content, it is impossible for users to flip from the home page every time.

Their more common behaviors are:

- Find a general direction first
- Then enter a more specific content list
- Then continue filtering, clicking, and jumping

At this time, the category page is actually the first-level navigation entrance for users.

### 2. Undertake more stable keywords

Many search terms are inherently suitable to be picked up by category pages.

for example:

- SEO tools
- AI writing tool
- Remote jobs
- Technical SEO tutorials
- Recommended running shoes

What these words have in common is:

- The theme is relatively stable
- Not a one-time content requirement
- Users often want to see a set of results first, rather than just one article

For this kind of word, category pages are often more suitable to handle than single content.

### 3. Organize site content into a sustainably scalable structure

A single article can only solve one point.

The category page solves one aspect.

Especially for websites whose content will continue to grow, category pages actually play a very important structural role:

- Tell users what the site is about
- Tell search engines which topics are updated over time
- String scattered content into clearer thematic paths

The problem is:

**The category page in the eyes of users is "what other content is under this topic"; the category page in the eyes of search engines is "is this a page worthy of being used as an official entrance?" **

These two things are not necessarily naturally consistent.

So what we really need to do is not to first ask "can the category page be included?" but to first clarify:

-Which category pages really have thematic value?
- Which are just groupings at the content management level
- Which category pages are worth focusing on?
-Which category pages only need to be used for site navigation?

## How to deal with category pages? I would suggest handling these 5 situations separately first.

### 1. A core category page with a clear theme, stable search demand, and sufficient content can be used as a formal SEO entrance.

Let’s talk about the conclusion first:

**Some category pages should be treated as key pages. **

for example:

- `/category/seo-tools`
- `/category/ai-writing`
- `/category/remote-jobs`
- `/category/technical-seo`
- `/category/running-shoes`

This type of category page usually has several characteristics:

- The category name itself is the word that users will search for
- Topic boundaries are relatively clear
- There are constantly new content added to the page
- After the user enters, he can indeed continue to read further.

Don’t just treat this kind of category page as a backend column.

A more stable approach is usually:

- Don’t just write the category name in the title, try to bring a more natural search expression
- Add a short introduction at the beginning of the page to let users know what they can see here.
- Make internal link entrances for core categories and don’t bury them too deeply.
- Make sure the quality and quantity of content are sustainable and don’t go online in vain

To put it bluntly, the most truly valuable category page is essentially no longer a "randomly generated list page", but a long-term topic entry. **

---

### 2. The content is too little, the theme is too vague, and the category page is only built for convenient management. Don’t push it to grab search traffic.

There are also many such category pages.

It looks like there are columns on the surface, but they are easily empty when actually opened:

- Only 2 to 3 pieces of content
- Classification words are particularly broad, such as "experience", "sharing" and "information"
- There is nothing on the page, only the title and article list
- Users don’t know what problem this category is trying to solve after reading it.

The biggest trouble with this kind of page is not that "it exists", but that you always want it to take on tasks beyond its capabilities.

Just because the system can generate a category page does not mean that this category page is worthy of SEO.

If a category:

- The topic boundaries are unclear
- Insufficient accumulation of content
- There will be no stable updates in the future.
- No obvious search requirements

Then my advice is usually:

** First treat it as the management structure of the site. Don’t expect it to get traffic as soon as you start it. **

Many website category pages cannot be built, not because they are not optimized enough, but because the page itself does not have enough reason to exist.

---

### 3. When the classification boundaries overlap and the relationship between superiors and subordinates is confusing, clear the structure first, otherwise it will be easy for you to rob yourself no matter how you optimize it.

What I fear most about category pages is not the lack of them, but the chaos.

You can easily see this on some sites:

- SEO
- Technical SEO
- SEO optimization
- Website optimization
- SEO Tutorial
- Search engine optimization

These words all seem to be somewhat related, but if they are all classified into horizontal categories, a problem arises:

- Each category page is grabbing similar words
- Content is loaded repeatedly
- Users cannot understand the difference
- The editor himself doesn’t even know where to put the new content

At this time, if you change the title, add a description, and supplement the introduction, the help is actually limited.

Because the real problem with the page is not that it is “not written well enough”, but that the structure itself has no division of labor.

A more stable idea is usually:

- First clarify what major topics the first-level classification is responsible for
- What are the more detailed requirements that the second-level classification is responsible for?
- The tab page only complements the horizontal theme and does not replace the category page.
- Thematic pages handle clearer and more complete search intent

In one sentence:

**The category page first solves "who is responsible for which word", and then talks about "how to optimize this word". **

Otherwise, it will easily become internal friction among several columns.

---

### 4. If you really want the category page to participate in rankings, don’t just leave a title and a list. At least make it look like a serious entry page.

This is where many category pages are most likely to be lazy.

After opening, it usually looks like:

- an H1
- several article titles
- Page turn button
- No more

Whether this kind of page can be included may not be the biggest problem sometimes.

The bigger question is:

**Even if it is seen, it is difficult for users or search engines to think that this page is worthy of being saved, clicked, and recommended separately. **

If you really want to use certain category pages as key entrances, I recommend that you at least add these things:

- An introduction at the beginning of the page to clearly explain the topic scope of this category
- Appropriately select a few core contents, do not spread all the contents flat
- Make category page titles and descriptions closer to real search expressions
- Make clearer groupings when there are many categories
- Give users a clear path to continue browsing

You don’t have to make the category page into a super long article, but at least don’t make it like an empty template that the system spits out by default.

Many category pages cannot be built, not because the keywords are not chosen correctly, but because the page experience is too "backend-like".

---

### 5. Decide not to focus on the category pages, and unify the signals. Don’t let sitemap, internal links, canonical, and indexing strategies fight with each other.

This is also a very common follow-up question.

Many teams will handle it this way:

- Saying that some category pages are not important
- But it's still there in the sitemap
- The homepage, sidebar, and footer provide entrances everywhere.
- canonical randomly points to the homepage or parent page
- He even wants search engines to "take care of it" themselves

The final situation that is most likely to occur is:

- You think it's not important, but the signal on the site keeps amplifying it
- You don't want it to compete, but it's still consuming crawls and weights
- You want to concentrate the signal, but the pointing relationship between the pages is unclear.

A more stable approach is usually:

- Core category pages focus on retaining entrances
- Don’t enlarge secondary category pages everywhere in the site
- Prioritize the pages you really want to put in the sitemap
- canonical, internal linking, and indexing strategies make sense to each other

Don’t say “this page is not important” while giving it the best position on the site.

## The 4 most common SEO mistakes I see on category pages are more likely to mess up the site than not dealing with them.

### 1. The category names are all from an internal perspective, and users will not search like this at all.

For example, if you think "Growth Insights", "Solution Center" and "Competency Matrix" are very professional, users may not recognize them.

Category pages will eventually have to face real searches and real clicks, not for internal reporting.

### 2. Put everything into categories, and as a result, one content is listed in three or four columns at the same time.

This will directly blur the classification boundaries.

It seems that the content coverage is wider, but in fact the topic is becoming increasingly unclear.

### 3. All category pages have a template. Except for different words, everything else is almost the same.

This kind of page is easiest to make thinner and thinner, and it is also the most difficult to establish independent value.

### 4. There is no division of labor among category pages, tag pages, topic pages, and search pages. In the end, the entire site is grabbing the same batch of words.

This is not because there are many entrances, but because the structure is chaotic.

It’s not that many websites don’t have pages, but that the roles of the pages are not clearly defined.

## If you want to check the category page settings of the website now, I recommend going through them in this order.

### Step one: First pull out all category pages and look at the topics, content volume and traffic performance

Highlights:

-Which category pages have enough content?
-Which category pages have not been updated for a long time?
-Which classifiers themselves have search requirements?
- Which category pages have almost no clicks and inclusions

### Step 2: Draw out core categories and non-core categories

The core categories give priority to meeting the following criteria:

- The topic is clear
- Users will search
-Content can be continuously supplemented
- Worth long-term operation

### Step 3: Merge or clean up overlapping classifications, overly detailed classifications, and borderless classifications

If the structure is not smooth, all subsequent optimizations will be very laborious.

Many times, a few less category pages are more useful than dozens of empty category pages.

### Step 4: Supplement page content and internal link entrances for core categories

Don’t just change the title.

What really affects the effect is often:

- Is the theme of the page clearly stated?
- Is the page content sufficient to support the theme?
- Is it regarded as a key entrance in the site?

### Step 5: Unify index and site signals, and then look at subsequent data changes

Key observations:

-Does the core category page receive search traffic stably?
- Do overlapping category pages continue to compete for keywords?
- Is crawling and inclusion more focused on key pages?
- Is the division of labor between category pages, tag pages, and topic pages clearer?

## Last sentence

The real difficulty with category pages is never “whether to do it or not”, but whether you have thought clearly about what it is responsible for in the entire site structure.

It is neither possible to generate traffic by just generating a column, nor should all category pages be turned off across the board.

Many websites cannot build category pages, not because the form of category pages is not good, but because:

- The theme is not clear
- The structure is not straightened out
- Page Space
- The signal is too messy
- Roles are mixed with tab pages and topic pages

So if you are still struggling with **how to deal with the category page**, my suggestion is just one sentence:

**First pick out the core category pages that are really worth building, and treat them as official entrances. The other category pages will serve navigation honestly, and don’t try to force them to grab rankings. **
