## Why do many titles “look like SEO” but are actually not friendly at all?

I’ve seen too many people think first when writing titles:

"Keywords must be inserted."

Then the title slowly became like this:

**Which AI writing tool is recommended to use 2026 latest free version**

Do you think it has any keywords? have.

Can search engines understand it? Most likely so.

But when ordinary people see this kind of title, their first reaction is usually not "I want to click in and take a look" but "This looks too much like it was spelled out by a machine."

This is the pitfall that many people are most likely to fall into when doing SEO: they only focus on letting search engines recognize it, but forget that the last person who clicks is still the user. **

A truly useful SEO title is not to force keywords into it, but to allow keywords, search intent and click desire to be established at the same time.

To put it bluntly, the SEO-friendly title is just one sentence:

**Not only let search engines know what you are writing, but also make users feel that this article is worth clicking. **

---

## How do I tell whether a title is good or not?

When I look at titles, I don’t just look at “are there keywords?” but first go through these 4 questions:

1. **When users search for this word, what do they really want to see? **
2. **Can the first half of the title immediately convey the topic? **
3. **Are there any concrete benefits in the title, rather than just empty words? **
4. **If I remove the website name, will this title still make me think about it? **

If two of these four questions cannot be answered, the title will most likely have to be revised.

I later developed a very practical habit: write 3 versions of the same topic first, and then show it to different models. For example, I will directly use **AI to compare** and see how Claude, GPT, and DeepSeek will change the title. Not copying, but using them to see the perspective. Many times, what is really useful is not the answer directly given by AI, but the fact that it reminds you:

**Oh, it turns out that this title can be more human-like. **

## Method 1: Determine the "search intent" first, then write the title, not the other way around

Many people write titles by first thinking of a catchy phrase and then inserting keywords into it.

The order is actually reversed.

A more correct approach is: **First determine what the user is looking for when searching for this word, and then decide what the title looks like. **

For example, the same "How to write an SEO title" may have three completely different intentions behind it:

- Want to learn basic methods
- Want to see ready-made templates
- Want to solve the problem of "ranking but no clicks"

If your article is about practical templates, don't write the title as "How to write an SEO title" that is too general.

More directly:

- **Common title**: How to write SEO title
- **Better Titles**: How to write SEO titles? 7 practical methods that can be directly applied
- **More focused title**: How to write an SEO title? The 7 templates I commonly use have significantly higher click-through rates after modification.

The closer the title is to the real needs behind the search, the easier it will be for search engines to match, and the easier it will be for users.

## Method 2: Put the core keywords in the first half of the sentence, but don’t write the title as a keyword warehouse

The "keyword prefix" thing is right.

But many people understand it as "the more keywords, the better", which starts to go astray.

What you really should do is: **Put the core keywords in the first half of the title where they are most likely to be scanned, and then leave a little space for people to read. **

for example:

- **Not Recommended**: Sharing of the latest measured SEO-friendly title writing methods and SEO title optimization techniques in 2026
- **Recommended**: How to write an SEO-friendly title? These 7 modification methods are more effective than stacking keywords

The problem with the former is not that there are no keywords, but that it is too full, too crowded, and too much like handing in homework.

The benefits of the latter are:

- Let’s explain the topic at the beginning: How to write an SEO-friendly title
- The second half of the sentence gives the reason for clicking: 7 changes to make it more effective

Search engines and users each get the information they want, and only this kind of title can be typed.

## Method 3: Use less “comprehensive analysis” and “ultimate guide” and more specific results

I used to love writing words like this:

- Comprehensive analysis
- The most complete tutorial
- Ultimate Guide
- Complete guide

Later, I discovered that it’s not that this kind of word cannot be used, but that it is too general now, and it is difficult to support the desire to click on it alone. **

What users really care about is usually not "your article is very comprehensive", but "what can I get when I click on it".

Look at the comparison below:

- **Common Titles**: SEO Title Optimization Guide
- **Better Titles**: SEO Title Optimization Guide: 7 Changes to Make Your Articles More Clickable

Another example:

- **Common Title**: AI Writing Title Tips
- **Better Titles**: How to write AI writing titles? This change will make searching and clicking more comfortable

Concrete results are more convincing than big words.

If you really don’t know how to write the second half of the sentence, a simple idea is to add any one of these three types of information:

- **What problem can be solved**
- **What results can it bring**
- **Suitable for anyone, just follow the instructions**

## Method 4: Numbers are useful, but don’t make up numbers just for the sake of numbers.

Numbers do work, no doubt about that.

Because numbers naturally make the title more specific and more like “I can click on it and get a list.”

So these titles are usually easier:

- 5 ways
- 7 templates
- 10 tips
- 3 common mistakes

But the problem is that in order to add numbers, many people break the article into "7 points" and "10 points", and the result is that every point is watery.

This kind of title may attract clicks in the short term, but it will damage trust in the content in the long term.

My advice is simple:

**First look at the content and it will naturally fit into several points, and then decide how many numbers to write. **

If there are really 4 key methods in your article, just write 4 honestly. Don’t forcefully write 8 just to look good.

Users are not fools. The title says "10 tips", but when you click on it, you find that the first 6 are nonsense, so he won't click on you next time.

## Method 5: Question sentences are suitable for "question-type keywords", and conclusion-type titles are suitable for "decision-type keywords"

Not all titles are suitable for questions.

The advantage of questions is that they can create a sense of substitution, which is especially suitable for the following searches:

- How to write an SEO title
- Why is the title not clicked?
- Why do keywords have rankings but no traffic?
- How to avoid AI flavor when writing titles with AI

At this time, it will be more natural for you to use questions:

- How to write an SEO title? Don’t rush to pile up keywords first
- Why does your title rank but no one clicks on it? The problem may lie in these 3 places

But if the user originally searched for decision-making words, such as:

- The best AI writing tool
- Recommended SEO title optimization tools
- free title generator

In this case, conclusion titles are usually more direct:

- Recommended SEO title optimization tools: 5 practical tools for content teams
- What are the free title generators? I actually use these 5 more easily.

Don’t write all your titles in the same mold.

**First check whether the keyword behind it is "want to ask a question" or "want to make a choice", and then decide the tone of the title. **

## Method 6: Put the really important information in the first 30 words as much as possible

Many people like to pave the way when writing titles. They go around the front for a long time and focus on the back.

But judging from the display logic of the search results page, especially on mobile phones, the first half of the sentence is the most valuable position.

If your key information is placed in the second half and is cut off before the user can see it, half of the title will be written in vain.

So when I change the title now, I will give priority to ensuring that these two things are clearly explained in the first half of the sentence:

- **What is this content about**
- **Why it’s worth ordering**

For example:

- **Not good enough**: When making content, I found that many people don’t know how to write SEO-friendly titles. Let’s talk about a few methods today.
- **Better**: How to write an SEO-friendly title? These 7 methods are more effective than stacking keywords

The second title almost explains the theme and value in the first half of the sentence.

That's the difference.

## Method 7: Before sending it out, rewrite at least 3 versions before choosing

Many people cannot write titles well, not because of lack of ability, but because they only write one page and then publish it.

For something like a title, the first reaction is often just "it works", not necessarily "the best".

When I write articles now, I usually prepare at least 3 versions:

### Version A: the most standard keyword type
- How to write an SEO friendly title? 7 practical methods to share

### Version B: More emphasis on results
- How to write an SEO friendly title? If you change this way, the click-through rate of the article will usually be better.

### Version C: More emphasis on pain points
- SEO titles always get no clicks? The problem may not be the content, but that the title is not written correctly.

Then look at which one is more suitable for the article itself.

If it’s a partial tutorial, choose A;
If it’s more about experience sharing, choose B;
If it’s more about problem solving, choose C.

You can even show these 3 versions to your colleagues and ask:

**"Just looking at the title, which one do you want to order the most?"**

Many times, the intuition of bystanders is more accurate than your own.

## When doing SEO titles in 2026, I think the three most noteworthy changes are

### 1. Search engines are increasingly able to recognize “natural expressions”
In the past, many people worried that if the words in the title were not written exactly the same, it would be detrimental to SEO.

It's not so rigid now.

As long as the theme is clear, the semantics are close, and the content matches, many natural expressions can still achieve good performance.

### 2. Intention to click will become increasingly important
If there are exposures but no clicks, it will be difficult to stabilize your rankings in the long term.

Therefore, in the future, titles must not only be "searched", but also "willing to be clicked".

### 3. AI will make title production faster, but also easier to homogenize
Now everyone can use AI to generate titles in batches, which is a good thing in itself.

But the side effects are also obvious: **More and more titles look like the same mold. **

So you have to make your own judgment and don’t just send whatever the AI ​​gives you.

AI is suitable for helping you spread the word, but in the end, you still have to make the decision yourself.
