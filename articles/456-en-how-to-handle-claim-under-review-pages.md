## Why do many cross-border e-commerce, independent websites, and DTC brand websites go through the claims, appeals, replacement parts, and after-sales review processes? In the end, the most likely thing to be caught by search engines is not the help page, but a series of review pages, under-review URLs, and pending-review pages?

I've seen a lot of this type of problem lately.

Many teams have worked very hard on product pages, category pages, blog pages, and FAQ pages. As a result, when it comes to claims review, manual review, replacement parts queuing, and after-sales processing, a batch of "relevant URLs under review" will grow in the back of the system, such as:

- `/claim/under-review`
- `/claim/in-review`
- `/appeal/pending-review`
- `/after-sales/review-status`
- `/case/waiting-review`
- `/merchant/claim/review-record`

When many people see this kind of page for the first time, several natural judgments will pop up in their minds:

- Users would originally search for “What should I do during a claim review?”
- The page has status, time, and progress prompts, and does not look like an empty page.
- Now that the system has generated it, let’s leave it alone for now. Maybe we can also pick up some long-tail words.
- Compared with the "close page" and "reject page", the page under review looks more normal and seems to be more suitable for inclusion.

None of these ideas sound outrageous.

But once the site has been running for a long time, you will usually find that what really biases SEO is often not the content pages you carefully wrote, but the process pages that no one has sorted out alone: ​​they are highly dependent on specific orders, specific cases, specific accounts, specific review nodes and specific timeliness. When search users click in, the first thing they often see is "The current case is under review, please wait patiently", "Only the current account can view the complete review status", "Supplementary materials have been submitted, waiting for manual review" or "The review results will be notified through on-site messages."

In the end, what the search engine grabbed was a bunch of private process URLs. The explanation pages, FAQ pages, and rules pages that should really be responsible for "How long do you have to wait during the claim review?" "Why does it always show that it is under review?" "Can I still add materials during the review?" Instead, they didn't do it.

So if you are also reading **How to deal with pages in the review** recently, I will put the conclusion first:

**Most of the pages under review are essentially process status pages, progress reminder pages and internal processing pages, not public search landing pages. It is certainly valuable for users to check progress, customer service diversion, and operation troubleshooting, but most of the time it is not suitable to be promoted as a key SEO page. The key is not whether there are three words "under review" on the page, nor whether there is a progress bar, but to distinguish first: whether this URL is serving a specific case, or whether it is answering an open, stable, and reusable search question. **

## What problem is the page under review solving? Its priority is to serve people in the process, not people who search for the first time.

### 1. It prioritizes serving people who are already in the claims process.

The most common functions of the review page are generally:

- Tell the user that the case has been submitted successfully and is waiting for review
- Tell the user whether the current card is in preliminary review, review, manual inspection or carrier confirmation
- Tell the user whether they should continue to wait, replenish materials, or contact customer service at this time
- Tell customer service and operations which node the case is at now, whether it has timed out, and whether manual intervention is required.

In other words, it serves people who already have the context, rather than an unfamiliar user who clicks in from Google for the first time.

### 2. It is usually deeply tied to the case number, account identity, review node, and processing time limit.

In an audit page, it is common to have this information:

- Order number
- Claim number
- Current review status
- Submission time
- Estimated processing time
- Materials currently to be replenished
- Whether to allow resubmission
-Current account permissions

This determines that the pages under review are usually not stable, standardized, and suitable for long-term search traffic.

### 3. It has business value, but it does not mean it has SEO value.

The most common things that many teams are confused about are the following things:

- Pages are important in the process, so they should also be important in SEO
- There are status descriptions and time reminders on the page, so it is worth including
- Users will definitely see it, so search engines should see it too

But what search users will really search for is often:

-How long does it usually take for a claim to be reviewed?
- Why does it always show that it is under review?
- Can I still add materials during the review?
- What is the difference between under review, under review, and on hold?
- Is there something wrong if there is no result from the review for a long time?

What is truly more suitable to handle these search needs is usually not a private review page, but:

- Claims review time limit explanation page
- Frequently asked questions during review FAQ page
- Review status explanation page
- Waiting for review rules page after supplement
- Audit timeout troubleshooting page

## How to deal with pages under review? I prefer to handle these 5 situations separately first

### 1. If it is just a standard review page, a page in processing, or a page to be reviewed, there is usually no need to treat it as a key SEO page.

This is the most common category.

Most of these pages have several things in common:

- You must log in to view
- Must bind case number or user identity
- The content of the page focuses on a specific claim record
- The status will continue to change as the review progresses, supplements are made, and manual processing takes place.
- There is almost no stable reading value for external users

In a word: **The standard review page is more like a process page, not a public content page. **

### 2. If the real search value is "How long does it take for the results to be released during the review" and "Why is it always under review", don't let the private review page take over the traffic for these questions.

Many teams tend to make mistakes here.

Because users do search for "under review", "pending review" and "under review", it is natural to draw a conclusion: since there are people searching for it, the under review page should be included.

But what users really want to know is usually not:

- Which auditor is assigned to a certain case?
- Why does an account only show part of its status this time?
- Was a certain record just transferred from preliminary review to review yesterday?
- Is it possible for a certain merchant to click the supplement button in the backend now?

What they want to know more is:

- How long is the normal review time?
- Why has the review taken so long and there is still no result?
- Can I still add information during the review?
- Under what circumstances should you proactively contact customer service?
- What is the difference between under review, pending and review?

This type of request is more suitable for the public description page, help page, FAQ page and rules page, rather than the review page of a specific case.

### 3. If the system will generate variant URLs such as under-review, in-review, pending-review, review-status, and review-record at the same time, be sure to control them together.

The real problem with many websites is never just a `/claim/under-review`.

More commonly, the system will also grow:

- `/claim/under-review`
- `/claim/in-review?id=xxx`
- `/appeal/pending-review`
- `/case/waiting-review`
- `/after-sales/review-status`
- `/merchant/claim/review-record`
- `/claim/review-history`
- `/support/case/in-review-detail`

Once there are many such URLs, it is easy to appear:

-The main body of the content is almost the same
- Only the parameters, nodes, and time are different
- Title templates are highly repetitive
- Pages such as record, history, and detail are also captured.
- The user center, merchant backend, and site messages continue to provide entrances to these pages.

Therefore, when dealing with pages in the review, a very practical action is:

**Don’t just focus on the main review page, look at the status record page, history page, details page, and processing page together. **

### 4. If you have both a "public description page" and a "private review page", be sure to completely separate these two types of pages.

Many websites are really messy, not because there are too few pages, but because the roles of the pages are not clearly defined.

One set is a public page, such as:

- How long does it usually take to get the results of a claims review?
- Can I still add materials during the review?
- What is the difference between under review, review, and pending?
- What to do after the review times out?
- Why hasn’t the status been updated?

The other set is private pages, such as:

- The review status page of an order
- The audit record page of a certain merchant's backend
- Details page to be reviewed after a certain supplement
- A manual review progress page exclusive to an account

If these two types of pages are mixed together, the most common result is:

- No one accepts the public demand
- The private page was crawled by search engines
- Users come in from search and cannot understand and cannot see complete information.
- The description page and FAQ page that really deserve the ranking cannot be opened.

### 5. If you do not intend to allow the pages in the review to participate in the ranking, unify noindex, login interception, sitemap, canonical and internal links together

A lot of mid-page SEO issues in an audit are not because “there is this page” but because the signals are all fighting.

Common situations include:

- Noindex is written on the page template, but the sitemap continues to submit the under-review URL
- The main page requires login, but pages such as history, detail, and record can also be publicly accessed.
- canonical points to confusion, and the pages under review, pending pages, and review pages are intertwined with each other.
- The user center, help center, and site search are still making crawlable entrances to these pages.

If you have determined that the page in the review should not be a key SEO entry, then don’t do it half way.

A more stable approach is usually:

- Unify the page level with noindex or access after complete login
- sitemap does not submit such process URLs
- canonical Don’t point to intermediate status pages randomly
- Do not package the review page into a content page for internal links
- History, detail, record, and parameter pages become crawlable entries silently

## The 4 most common SEO mistakes I see on the mid-page audit are more likely to mess up the site than not doing them.

### 1. It is believed that “under review” means that users are capable of searching, so pages under review are naturally worthy of inclusion.

Users will search "Why is it always under review", which does not mean that the under-review URL of a specific case is worthy of ranking. The former is a public issue, the latter is usually just private process state.

### 2. Mix up the review page, review page, pending page, and result page. In the end, there will be a bunch of pages with similar semantics on the site.

The business meanings of these pages are inherently different. If the titles, templates, and internal links are all mixed up, in the end, they usually end up not covering more completely, but competing for words from each other.

### 3. Pages such as history, record, detail, and progress are easily released by the system. As a result, thin pages are placed one layer inside another.

These pages may be useful for internal processes, but are mostly of no independent value to public searches. Once released in batches, it spreads the crawl budget and creates duplicate content.

### 4. We should clearly make public content about "how long does it take to get results during claims review", but we always want to make the private process page handle the search demand.

This is actually the fundamental problem of many websites: it’s not that there are no user problems, but that these problems have never been compiled into public content.

## If you want to check the website's mid-review page settings now, I recommend going through them in this order.

### Step one: First find out all relevant URLs under review

At least pull out these types and have a look:

- Page under review
- Pending review page
- review-status page
- history/record/detail page
- under-review URL with parameters
- Review process page in the merchant backend and user center

### Step 2: Determine which requirements should be handled by the public content page

Focus on what users are actually searching for:

-How long does it usually take for a claim to be reviewed?
- Why does it always show that it is under review?
- Can I still add materials during the review?
- What is the difference between under review, pending and review?
- What to do after the review times out

### Step 3: Completely separate the public description page and the private process page

Those that can handle searches will be made into description pages, FAQ pages, and help pages; those that can only serve specific cases will be managed as process pages.

### Step 4: Unify technical signals, don’t let the page continue to be pushed for crawling while it is noindexed

Look at noindex, canonical, sitemap, login interception, and internal link entrance together. Don’t just change one point and think it’s over.

### Step 5: When looking at the results, don’t just look at “whether the page under review has been included”

What you should look at more is:

- Has the public description page started accepting search terms such as "how long does it take for results to be released during review?"
- Are process pages such as under-review, record, and history gradually exiting the index?
- Does the crawl budget return to the product page, category page, blog page and help page?
- After searching for users, can they find really useful answers faster?
