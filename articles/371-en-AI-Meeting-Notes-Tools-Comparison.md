## Why are more and more people starting to use AI meeting minutes tools?

Because when it comes to meetings, what is really tiring is often not the hour of "opening", but the bunch of finishing touches after the meeting.

Who said what, what is the conclusion, who took which task, and whether to follow up next week - if these things are organized manually, there are basically three consequences: incomplete memory, inaccurate memory, and no one will read it after recording. **

Especially now, many teams have meetings that are fragmented and intensive: morning meetings, weekly meetings, project synchronization meetings, customer communication meetings, and review meetings. You just closed the Tencent meeting earlier, and you have to enter Feishu again later; the meeting still contains Chinese and English, product terminology, and questions that the boss added temporarily. Once there are too many meetings, the minutes can easily become "written in form, but no one can actually implement it."

This is why AI meeting minutes tools are so popular recently. They no longer just help you convert recordings into text, but have begun to move in a more practical direction: ** Automatically extract key points, automatically divide into action items, automatically generate summaries, and automatically recall original sentences. **

I recently tried five of the more common AI meeting minutes tools to see if they can really help, or if they are just repackaging "recording to text".

---

## How did I test?

This time I didn’t just use a quiet paragraph of standard Mandarin to test, because real meetings are not like that at all.

I have prepared 3 sets of materials that are closer to real office scenes:

1. **30-minute Chinese Weekly Meeting Recording**: Test Chinese recognition, spoken word filtering, and key refining abilities
2. **Chinese-English product seminar**: Test the extraction of English terms, names, brand names and action items
3. **Multi-person remote conference screen recording**: Test whether speaker distinction, meeting summary, and to-do splitting are reliable

I focus on 5 dimensions:

- **Is the transcription accurate**
- **Does the summary look like it was written by a human**
- **The to-do list is not clear**
- **Does it work well with conferencing software and document tools**
- **It is inconvenient to review and search after the meeting**

The problem with many tools is not that they cannot be converted, but that they convert a lot of words and you still have to reorganize them yourself. If you end up having to manually rewrite the minutes, it doesn't really save you time.

## 1. Feishu Miaoji: If you are already working in Feishu, it is really trouble-free.

**Usage experience:**

The biggest advantage of Feishu Miaoji is actually not that a single ability is particularly popular, but that it is too close to your daily work.

Many teams already use Feishu to hold meetings, send messages, write documents, and divide tasks. After the meeting, there is basically no need to cut out the entire link of recording, transcribing, taking minutes, sharing, and continuing collaboration. For domestic teams, this "smoothness" itself is a great productivity.

I tried it and found that its recognition of Chinese meetings is generally relatively stable, especially in scenarios such as daily weekly meetings and project synchronization meetings. Its ability to refine key points is sufficient. Not every sentence is perfect, but at least you can quickly pull out a readable version, and then send it to the team with a little smoothing.

It is more suitable for teams with a fast pace and frequent collaboration: as soon as the meeting ends, the minutes are immediately entered into the document, and relevant people follow up immediately. At this time, you will find that the value of the tool depends not only on the recognition rate, but also on whether you can continue working after the meeting.

**Suitable scene:**
- A team focused on Feishu meetings and Feishu documents
- Chinese weekly meetings, project meetings, review meetings
- People who need to synchronize documents while having meetings
- Teams who want to directly connect minutes into the collaboration process

**Disadvantages:**
- If the team does not use Feishu, the advantages will be reduced
- Cross-platform workflow is not as flexible as third-party tools
- English conferences are not its most advantageous home venue

**My suggestion:** If your company is already in the Feishu ecosystem, you can basically try Feishu Miaoji first, and it will not be easy to make detours.

---

## 2. Otter.ai: The English conference is very mature, but it is more focused on the logic of overseas teams.

**Usage experience:**

Otter.ai has been a veteran player in the field of AI conference minutes over the years, and its strengths are clear: **English conferences are really stable. **

If your work often involves English meetings, cross-border communications, and interviews with overseas customers, Otter's real-time transcription experience will be more mature than many general-purpose tools. The functions of speaker separation, real-time subtitles, and post-meeting summaries are also relatively complete and do not look like they were put together temporarily.

But the feeling it gives me is also very obvious: this is a product typically made according to the habits of overseas teams. The logic is clear and the functions are mature, but for Chinese users, some details may not be the smoothest. For example, oral expressions in the Chinese context and meeting content mixed with English abbreviations are not as natural as native tools.

**Suitable scene:**
- Meeting minutes in English
- Communication with overseas customers
- Remote multinational team synchronization
- People who need real-time subtitle assistance

**Disadvantages:**
- The Chinese scene performance is not the strongest
- General support for domestic office ecology
- Price and subscription logic are more biased towards the overseas SaaS route

**My suggestion:** If you mainly hold **English meetings**, Otter.ai is still worth trying seriously; but if you spend most of your time in a Chinese office environment, it may not be the most convenient one.

---

## 3. Fireflies.ai: Very suitable for teams that “catch up on the action after the meeting”

**Usage experience:**

Fireflies.ai doesn’t feel “most like a meeting note taker” to me, but more like a **post-meeting follow-up assistant**.

It not only helps you remember the content, but also actively organizes key points, breaks down action items, and categorizes discussion topics. This is actually particularly important for positions such as sales, customer success, and project management that rely heavily on follow-up. Many times, the real value of meeting minutes is not that they are fully recorded, but that it is clear who will do what next.

In addition, its integration capabilities are relatively strong and can be connected with many common office tools. This can be very useful for teams with scattered tool stacks. You don't need to trap the meeting content in an isolated page, but can continue to flow into the CRM, task system, and knowledge base.

**Suitable scene:**
- Sales calls, client meetings
- Teams who need to track action items
- Teams with more tools and longer processes
- People who want to connect minutes with business systems

**Disadvantages:**
- The configuration is a bit too much for the first time.
- The Chinese experience is not as relevant as local tools
- Some advanced features are available in the paid version

**My suggestion:** If the core purpose of your meeting is not to "keep files" but to **promote things after the meeting**, Fireflies.ai will be more valuable than many tools that only transcribe.

## 4. Notta: Individual users and light teams will find it very practical

**Usage experience:**

The advantages of Notta are simple: it can be used as soon as you open it, and the recording and transcription is relatively stable. **

It does not have a particularly strong platform temperament, nor does it have a heavy corporate backend feel, but is rather friendly to individual users. After you throw in interviews, interviews, internal discussions, and group meetings, you can usually get an organized transcript and summary relatively quickly.

I tried it, and it is particularly suitable for the scenario of "I get the content right first, and then decide how to use it later." For example, if you are a content team, researcher, consultant, reporter, or you just often need to organize interview data, a pragmatic tool like Notta will make people feel comfortable.

**Suitable scene:**
- Interview transcription
- Minutes of small team meetings
- Content interview arrangement
- Personal knowledge organization

**Disadvantages:**
- Collaboration closed loop is not as good as ecological tools like Feishu
- Action item management is not the strongest point
- More focused on “arranging materials” rather than “driving processes”

**My suggestion:** If what you care about most is **stable transcoding, easy to use, and can be used by just one person**, Notta is worth putting on the shortlist.

## 5. tl;dv: suitable for remote teams to review the key points of the meeting, not suitable for only viewing Chinese transcriptions

**Usage experience:**

tl; What attracts me most about tools like dv is that it does not simply turn a meeting into a text, but puts more emphasis on **meeting playback, key positioning and fragment reuse**.

This is especially useful for product teams, remote teams, and people collaborating across time zones. Not everyone can come to the meeting, but everyone can quickly jump to the key clips and watch the key discussions directly, instead of dragging the one-hour screen recording from beginning to end.

It does a good job of "what places are worth reviewing" and is also suitable for organizing content such as user interviews, product demonstrations, and team synchronization. However, if what you care about most is Chinese transliteration and local office habits, then its advantages are not so obvious.

**Suitable scene:**
-Review of remote team meetings
- Product interviews and user research
- Collaborate across time zones
- People who need to quickly locate key clips

**Disadvantages:**
- The Chinese experience is not as good as tools like Feishu Miaoji and Notta
- More of a remote collaboration approach, not a traditional note-taking route
- Domestic teams need to adapt to access habits

**My Advice:** If your team regularly meets remotely and someone isn’t able to attend the whole time, the value of tl;dv suddenly becomes very high.

## How will AI meeting minutes tools change in 2026?

My own judgment is that there will be four particularly obvious directions:

1. **Meeting minutes will change from "recording tool" to "execution portal"**
   In the future, instead of just getting a document and ending the meeting, the to-do list, person in charge, and deadline will be synchronized directly.

2. **Multi-language conferencing capabilities will become increasingly important**
   Especially for overseas teams and multinational teams, whoever can handle the mixed Chinese and English, accent, and terminology more stably will be more competitive.

3. **The focus is no longer just on the summary, but on “trackability”**
   It is no longer enough to write a beautifully written summary. Everyone will be more concerned about whether the tasks have been missed and whether the controversial points can be traced back.

4. **Video playback and knowledge accumulation will be tied more tightly**
   Many meetings in the future will not only be for current synchronization, but will also become part of the team's knowledge base.
