## Why are so many people starting to use AI mind mapping now?

To put it bluntly, it's not that everyone suddenly fell in love with "mind mapping", but that the things in the mind are becoming more and more complicated.

When making a plan, you need to outline a structure, when writing an article, you need to build a framework, when preparing for an interview, you need to sort out knowledge points, and you need to explain your ideas clearly before the meeting. In the past, people used to stare at blank pages in a daze, but now many people have changed their approach: first throw the theme to AI, let it give you a framework, and then add things to it yourself.

This idea is particularly suitable for two types of people:

- **People who have a bunch of ideas in their mind, but don’t know where to start**
- **People are pressed for time and need to draw up an outline quickly**

I recently tried five of the more common AI mind mapping tools in a row to see if they can really improve performance, or if they just look smart.

---

## How did I test?

I didn’t bother them with particularly academic questions, but used a very down-to-earth scenario:

**Test Topic**:
"Help me make a mind map of "Independent Developers' AI Plug-in Growth Plan", including customer acquisition channels, content operations, product optimization, user conversion, data review and next week's action items."

I mainly look at 5 things:

1. **Is the generation speed fast**?
2. **Does the structure look the same**
3. **Can you understand Chinese easily**
4. **Follow-up editing is not troublesome**
5. **Is export and collaboration easy to use**

Many tools look similar at first glance, but they are very different once you get started. Some are generated quickly, but the content is empty; some have a complete structure, but it is difficult to modify; others are suitable for team meetings, but not suitable for individuals to organize casually.

## 1. XMind AI: The one most suitable for “handing in homework”

**Usage experience:**

If you have used XMind before, there is almost no barrier to getting started. Its AI function is not that fancy, but rather stable - the generated structure is usually more regular, and the hierarchy is more like that of a person who has actually done something.

After I threw in the above growth plan question, the framework provided by XMind AI is the most "can be taken directly to a meeting" among the five. Not the one that blows the best, but it does look the most like the finished product.

Especially its layout, color matching, and node layout, there is no need to clean up the appearance after finishing it. This is important for scenarios you want to show your boss, clients, and colleagues.

**Suitable scene:**
-Write project plan
- Organize the framework before reporting
- Make courses, training, and knowledge system diagrams
- Need to be exported into a formal file

**Disadvantages:**
- The free quota is too small
- AI function is more suitable for "forming first and then fine-tuning", and the flexible brainstorming feeling is not so strong
- Real-time team collaboration is not its core strength

**My suggestion:** If what you want is **stable, presentable, and able to meet people**, XMind AI is very suitable. It is easy to use it to make plans and reviews by yourself.

---

## 2. GitMind: Chinese users basically don’t step on thunder

**Usage experience:**

The biggest advantage of GitMind is just four words: **worry-free and easy**.

It has a good understanding of Chinese prompt words and does not require you to write a sentence as rigorously as writing a paper. You can enter a topic casually, and it will basically give you a framework that can still be viewed. For domestic users, the access speed, interface habits, and functional logic are relatively friendly.

In my actual use, GitMind is very suitable for the working method of "build it first and then slowly change it". For example, writing outlines for weekly reports, planning topic selections, and preparing exam review charts can quickly lay a foundation for these.

**Suitable scene:**
- Study review
- Writing outline
- Work breakdown
- Daily brainstorming

**Disadvantages:**
- The depth expansion under complex structures is not so amazing
- Personalized style is not particularly strong
- Some advanced features require membership

**My suggestion:** If you are coming into contact with AI mind mapping for the first time, **it is safe to start with GitMind**. You don’t have to worry about it, and you won’t be easily dissuaded by the complicated interface.

---

## 3. Miro AI: It is really useful when a group of people think about things together.

**Usage experience:**

Miro AI is not the kind of tool that "one person writes an outline and leaves", it is more like a **team co-creation whiteboard**.

If your usage scenario is for several people to hold meetings together, add nodes together, and discuss processes together, then Miro's advantages will be particularly obvious. You can let the AI ​​​​generate a version of the framework while letting colleagues add ideas online. The whole process is very suitable for remote collaboration.

Its strongest point is not that "the AI ​​is much better than others", but that after it is generated, everyone can continue to work together. This is very valuable in product, operations, consulting, and training teams.

**Suitable scene:**
-Team brainstorming
- Remote meetings
- Organizing user journey maps and flow charts
- Cross-department collaboration

**Disadvantages:**
- When you open it for the first time, you may feel that the amount of information is a bit large.
- The English environment is not friendly enough to some people
- Personally, it feels a bit heavy when used lightly.

**My suggestion:** If you are using it alone, Miro may not be the most trouble-free. But if your team often meets online, it can save a lot of time in back-and-forth alignment.

---

## 4. AmyMind: Suitable for people like "I want a picture now"

**Usage experience:**

AmyMind gives me the feeling: **No nonsense, just publish pictures**.

There is no particularly complicated learning cost. Just enter the topic, click, and the structure will come out quickly. This scenario is particularly suitable for making a simple outline, sorting out temporarily, and looking for ideas before writing.

What I like about it is that it doesn't give you too many distractions. Many tools want to make themselves "large and full-platform", and the result is a lot of functions, but you just want to draw a picture. AmyMind is more restrained and suitable for people who want to get to the point quickly.

**Suitable scene:**
- Personal inspiration organization
- The frontline framework for writing articles
- Sort out knowledge points before the interview
- Quickly prepare papers before meetings

**Disadvantages:**
- Average in-depth editing ability
-Team collaboration function is not outstanding
- Templates and extended gameplay are not that rich

**My advice:** If **fast** is what you value most, AmyMind is worth trying. Especially suitable for content creators, students, and freelancers.

---

## 5. TreeMind tree map: a very practical one in domestic office scenes

**Usage experience:**

TreeMind tree diagram is more like an "office-oriented" player.

It has many templates, making it easy to get started when making training courseware, reading notes, course outlines, and project dismantling. The support for Chinese scenes is also good, and it is unlikely that the translation will be heavy and the nodes will look awkward.

After trying it, I feel that its advantage is not that a single point is particularly explosive, but that it is relatively balanced overall. The speed is acceptable, there are enough templates, domestic access is stable, and it is suitable for most ordinary users.

**Suitable scene:**
- Arrangement of training courseware
- Teaching lesson preparation
- Project dismantling
- Daily office work of domestic team

**Disadvantages:**
- A very complex map, the details are not smooth enough
- Advanced collaboration and aesthetics are not top-notch
- If you are pursuing particularly professional output, you will still want to adjust it manually.

**My suggestion:** If you mainly work and study in China and want to find a tool with **no obvious shortcomings**, TreeMind can be put on the shortlist.

## How do I choose?

If you are too lazy to look at the parameters, just look at this:

- **Want to make a formal report and plan output** → Select **XMind AI**
- **First time using AI mind map, want to avoid pitfalls** → Select **GitMind**
- **Team meeting together to create** → Select **Miro AI**
- **Just want to clear my mind quickly** → Select **AmyMind**
- **There are many domestic office, training, and learning scenarios** → Select **TreeMind tree map**

My own practical advice is:

**Individual users give priority to "whether it is fast and smooth"; team users give priority to "whether collaboration saves trouble". **

Many people always want to find the "strongest" one at the beginning, but after using it, you will find that no tool can cover all scenarios. Whether you are writing plans, preparing for exams, doing training, or working together as a team, the answers are completely different.

## How will AI mind mapping tools change in 2026?

My own judgment is that there will be several trends next:

1. **From "generating maps" to "helping you advance tasks"**
   It not only gives you a picture, but also helps you break it down into to-dos, documents, forms, and presentations.

2. **Voice input will become more and more practical**
   The tool helps you organize it as you talk, which is especially useful for meeting notes and capturing ideas.

3. **Team collaboration and AI will be more closely tied**
   In the future, instead of you writing first and then collaborating, everyone will chat and generate together.

4. **Chinese scenes will become more detailed**
   Domestic tools have made rapid progress in the past two years, and scenarios such as exam preparation, reporting, teaching and training, and office work will become more and more practical.

## Write at the end

If you are still relying on Word header levels, Excel lists, and sticky notes to organize your ideas, to be honest, you are already a little tired.

The greatest value of AI mind mapping tools is not to think for you, but to help you sort out the messy threads in your mind first. Once you have the first version of the structure, subsequent modifications, deletions, and additions will be much easier.

My advice is simple:

**Choose one that is comfortable to use first and use it for a week. **

Don’t study all the features right from the start, and don’t worry about which one has the highest score. First use it in your real workflow - writing plans, doing reviews, preparing lessons, and preparing for interviews. Anything that can help you save time is a good tool.
