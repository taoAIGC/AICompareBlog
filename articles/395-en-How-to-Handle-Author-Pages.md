## Why do so many sites turn on multi-author mode and end up with empty author pages instead of useful traffic?

This happens all the time on blogs, news sites, SaaS knowledge bases, review sites, and media sites.

A CMS feature gets enabled, or a publishing workflow changes, and suddenly the site starts generating pages like:

- `/author/admin`
- `/author/editor-01`
- `/author/guest-post`
- `/author/marketing-team`

At first, teams often think this is a good thing:

- it looks more professional
- Google seems to care more about authorship
- maybe this helps with E-E-A-T
- maybe every author page can bring in extra search traffic

Then they look again a few weeks later and realize the pages usually look like this:

- just a name, avatar, and a list of posts
- many authors only published one article
- some “authors” are really just internal accounts
- there is no clear reason for a user to visit the page

That is when teams swing to one of two extremes:

- leave every author page open because “author signals are good for SEO”
- block all of them because “they are just thin archive pages”

Both approaches are too blunt.

So if you are trying to figure out **how to handle author pages**, here is the short version:

**Author pages do not automatically deserve indexation, and they do not automatically deserve removal either. What matters is whether the page represents a real author, a clear area of expertise, enough stable content, and enough standalone value to act like a useful author destination instead of a system-generated archive.**

---

## What problem do author pages actually solve? They are not just “post lists grouped by person”

A good author page can do at least three jobs.

### 1. It tells users who wrote the content

This matters even more in areas like:

- technical tutorials
- product reviews
- finance
- health
- legal content
- B2B advice

People increasingly want to know:

- who wrote this
- whether that person knows the topic
- what else they have written
- whether they are worth trusting

### 2. It helps organize expertise around a person

If one author consistently writes about the same subject, such as:

- technical SEO
- AI tools
- growth strategy
- product operations

then the author page can become a useful entry point for that body of work.

### 3. It adds visible ownership and accountability to a site

A lot of content feels anonymous.

A strong author page helps fix that by showing:

- a real author
- a stable publishing history
- a topic focus
- background, credentials, or experience when relevant

But that only works if the page actually looks like a real author page.

## How should you handle author pages? I’d split them into these 5 cases first.

### 1. Core authors with stable output and clear expertise can keep proper author pages

This is the strongest case.

If an author:

- publishes regularly
- covers a reasonably consistent topic set
- has enough content to justify a page
- adds trust to the brand

then that page can absolutely be treated as a real site asset.

A stronger version usually includes:

- a real bio
- topic focus or specialty
- featured or representative articles
- a short explanation of why readers should follow this author

In other words:

**A strong author page is not just a grouped archive. It is part of your credibility layer.**

### 2. One-off contributors, guest writers, and internal accounts do not always need indexable author pages

This is where many sites overdo it.

Just because a username exists in the CMS does not mean it deserves a public SEO page.

Typical weak cases include:

- an intern who published one article
- a guest contributor who wrote once
- a shared “editor” account
- a marketing team login used for publishing

These pages often add little value for users and create a lot of thin templates for search engines.

### 3. If the page only shows an avatar and a post list, do not expect much SEO value

This is the most common problem.

Many author pages say almost nothing about:

- who the person is
- why they are credible
- what they mainly write about
- where readers should start

If you want author pages to matter, give them more substance:

- a clear bio
- topic specialties
- selected articles
- background or credentials when appropriate
- useful browsing paths

### 4. Author pages, about pages, team pages, and expert pages need different jobs

Many sites create overlap between these templates.

They end up with multiple pages all trying to answer the same questions about the same person.

A cleaner split is:

- **author page**: what this person writes and what to read first
- **about page**: who the company or publication is
- **team page**: who is on the team
- **expert page**: credentials, authority, advisory role, or professional background

Do not let four different pages compete for the same identity signals.

### 5. If most author pages are not strategic, keep the signals consistent instead of half-opening them

A lot of sites say author pages are not important, but then they still:

- put all of them in the sitemap
- surface them everywhere in the template
- give them no real content
- leave them fully crawlable and indexable

That is how you end up with a large pile of weak pages.

A better approach is to separate:

- core author pages
- basic author attribution pages
- internal-only publishing accounts

Then align your sitemap, internal linking, and indexing rules with that decision.

## The 4 author-page SEO mistakes I see most often

### 1. Every CMS user automatically gets a public author page

A publishing account is not the same thing as a meaningful public identity.

### 2. The page has no useful author information but is expected to rank

If the page does not explain why the author matters, it is hard for users or search engines to care.

### 3. Too much content is published under generic names like admin or editor

That weakens trust and makes authorship feel vague.

### 4. The site talks about E-E-A-T but never updates author information

An old one-line bio is not a serious trust signal.

---

## If you want to audit your author pages now, go in this order

### Step 1: classify your authors

At minimum, separate:

- core recurring authors
- occasional contributors
- guest writers
- internal publishing accounts

### Step 2: check whether each page has standalone value

Look at:

- content volume
- topical consistency
- completeness of author info
- whether the page clearly explains what the author is known for

### Step 3: review how thin the template is

Check whether the page has:

- a real bio
- featured content
- expertise signals
- useful navigation beyond a plain post list

### Step 4: align site signals

Review:

- which author pages belong in the sitemap
- which ones deserve strong internal links
- whether author pages overlap with team or about pages
- whether low-value internal accounts are exposed publicly

### Step 5: watch real outcomes, not just page counts

The real question is:

- do strong author pages improve trust and engagement?
- are weak author pages creating thin-page drag?
- do readers continue into more content from the same person?

---

## Final thought

The hard part of author pages is not whether your CMS can generate them.

The hard part is deciding:

- which people are worth turning into public content hubs
- which pages build trust and which pages just repeat archives
- how author pages differ from about, team, and expert pages
- whether the page contains enough evidence to support the author identity

So if you are still wondering **how to handle author pages**, my advice is simple:

**Do not start by giving every account an indexable page. Start by identifying the authors who genuinely represent your content, then make those pages clear, useful, and credible.**
